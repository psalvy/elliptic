5.2 — Complex Multiplication (The Symmetry of the Gods)

For almost every choice of $\tau$, the lattice only has trivial symmetries (shifting by integers). But for very specific choices—like $\tau = i$ (a perfect square) or $\tau = e^{2\pi i / 3}$ (a perfect hexagon)—the lattice can be mapped perfectly onto itself via rotation and complex scaling.

This "extra" symmetry is called Complex Multiplication (CM). The profound consequence of CM is that the $j$-invariant for these special curves is no longer a messy decimal; it is an exact algebraic integer.

Srinivasa Ramanujan possessed an otherworldly intuition for these CM lattices. He looked at $\tau = \frac{1 + \sqrt{-58}}{2}$, calculated its massive integer invariant, and plugged it into the $q$-expansion of the modular forms from Module 4.

5.3 — The Ramanujan π Engine

By forcing the geometric invariant to equal a massive integer, Ramanujan manipulated the $q$-expansion ($q = e^{2\pi i \tau}$) to isolate the $\pi$ hidden in the exponent. This resulted in a formula of terrifying precision:

$$\frac{1}{\pi} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{9801} \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(4k)!(1103+26390k)}{(k!)^4 396^{4k}}$$
