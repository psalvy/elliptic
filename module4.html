The j-invariant & Modular Forms

4.1 — Fingerprinting Elliptic Curves

Different lattices can give isomorphic tori (just rescale or rotate). The $j$-invariant is the number that tells them apart. For a curve $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$:

$$j(E) = 1728 \cdot \frac{4a^3}{4a^3 + 27b^2}$$

Two elliptic curves over $\mathbb{C}$ are isomorphic if and only if they have the same $j$-invariant. Every value $j \in \mathbb{C}$ corresponds to some curve.

4.2 — The Upper Half-Plane

Instead of specifying a lattice by two generators $\omega_1, \omega_2$, we can normalize to $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\tau$ where $\tau = \omega_2/\omega_1$ lives in the upper half-plane $\mathbb{H} = \{\tau \in \mathbb{C} : \operatorname{Im}(\tau) > 0\}$.

The modular group $SL(2, \mathbb{Z})$ acts on $\mathbb{H}$: $\tau \mapsto \frac{a\tau + b}{c\tau + d}$. Two values of $\tau$ give the same torus iff they're related by this action. The fundamental domain is highlighted below.

4.4 — Modular Forms

A modular form of weight $k$ is a holomorphic function $f: \mathbb{H} \to \mathbb{C}$ satisfying:

$$f\!\left(\frac{a\tau+b}{c\tau+d}\right) = (c\tau+d)^k f(\tau)$$

The $j$-invariant is the unique modular function (weight 0) with a simple pole at the cusp $\tau \to i\infty$, normalized so $j(e^{2\pi i/3}) = 0$ and $j(i) = 1728$.

Its Fourier expansion (the "$q$-expansion", where $q = e^{2\pi i \tau}$):

$$j(\tau) = \frac{1}{q} + 744 + 196884q + 21493760q^2 + \cdots$$

The appearance of 196884 here connects to the Monster group — but that's a story for another course!

Key insight: The $j$-invariant converts the geometry of lattices into a single complex number. Special values of $j$ (algebraic integers) correspond to lattices with extra symmetry — these are the "CM curves" central to Module 5 and Ramanujan's work.
