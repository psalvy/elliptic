Demystifying Quotient Spaces

1.1 — The Simplest Case: $\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$

Before diving into complex tori, let's see the simplest quotient space: the real line modulo the integers. We declare $x \sim x + 1$ — any two points differing by an integer are "the same." The real line wraps into a circle.

1.2 — The 2D Lattice: $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$

Now move to the complex plane $\mathbb{C}$. Pick two complex numbers $\omega_1, \omega_2$ that aren't real multiples of each other. The lattice is:

$$\Lambda = \{m\omega_1 + n\omega_2 \mid m,n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$$

The quotient $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ identifies every point with all its translates by lattice vectors. Geometrically, a fundamental parallelogram with opposite edges glued. Drag the lattice generators below:

1.3 — Gluing: Parallelogram → Torus

The identification of edges turns a flat parallelogram into a torus. The slider controls two steps, and the shape is driven by $\omega_1, \omega_2$ above:

  1. 0–50%: Glue the orange ($\omega_2$) edges — roll the sheet into a cylinder. The tube radius comes from $|\omega_2|$.
  2. 50–100%: Bend the cylinder to glue the green ($\omega_1$) ends → a torus. The ring radius comes from $|\omega_1|$.

Try dragging $\omega_1, \omega_2$ above and watch the torus shape change — a longer $\omega_2$ relative to $\omega_1$ makes a fatter tube.

Key insight: The torus is not just a topological shape — it's the quotient space $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$, inheriting a flat metric from the plane. Two tori with different lattices are genuinely different geometric objects. This is the foundation of elliptic curve theory.
