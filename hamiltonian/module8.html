Lie Algebras & Lie Transformations

Module 7 left us with a linear map and a warning: real maps are nonlinear, and no clean quadratic invariant survives. Before we chase approximate invariants (Module 10), we take the other road — the one that builds a symplectic map out of a single generating function. The engine is an object we already met without naming it: the Poisson bracket of Module 4, now recognised as a Lie algebra. This is the language modern mechanics and accelerator physics are written in, and it is one of the two techniques the target paper leans on.

8.1 — The Poisson bracket is a Lie algebra

Recall the bracket from Module 4, which turned "how does $f$ change along the motion" into pure algebra:

$$\{f, g\} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial q}\frac{\partial g}{\partial p} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial p}\frac{\partial g}{\partial q}, \qquad \dot f = \{f, H\}.$$

Strip away the physics and look at what the bracket is as an operation on functions. It has exactly three properties:

  • Bilinear — linear in each slot: $\{af + bg, h\} = a\{f,h\} + b\{g,h\}$.
  • Antisymmetric — $\{f, g\} = -\{g, f\}$, so $\{f, f\} = 0$.
  • Jacobi identity — the bracket of brackets closes up:
    $$\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\} = 0.$$

Any vector space with a product obeying these three axioms is, by definition, a Lie algebra. The smooth functions on phase space form one — infinite-dimensional, with the Poisson bracket as its product. The whole apparatus of Lie theory, invented for continuous symmetry groups, applies verbatim to mechanics. And the atom of the whole structure is the bracket we computed in Module 4:

$$\{q, p\} = 1.$$

Quantum mechanics keeps this exact skeleton, swapping the bracket for a commutator: $\{f,g\}\to\tfrac{1}{i\hbar}[\hat f,\hat g]$, so $\{q,p\}=1$ becomes $[\hat q,\hat p]=i\hbar$. Same Lie algebra, different representation.

Key insight: The Poisson bracket is not just a formula for time evolution — it is a Lie bracket, making phase-space functions a Lie algebra. Every conserved quantity, every symmetry, every canonical transformation is a statement in that algebra.

8.2 — Every function generates a symplectic map

Fix a function $G$ and define the Lie operator $:\!G\!:$ — "bracket with $G$":

$$:\!G\!:\,f \;=\; \{G, f\}.$$

Then Hamilton's own equation $\dot f = \{H, f\}$ reads $\dot f = \,:\!H\!:\,f$, a linear ODE in the operator $:\!H\!:$. Its solution is a matrix-exponential-style series — the Lie transformation:

$$f(t) = e^{t\,:G:}\,f = f + t\{G,f\} + \frac{t^2}{2!}\{G,\{G,f\}\} + \cdots$$

Applied to the coordinates themselves, $e^{t:G:}$ is a symplectic map: it flows every point along the vector field generated by $G$,

$$X_G = \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial p},\; -\frac{\partial G}{\partial q}\right),$$

and because it is a Hamiltonian flow it preserves area automatically (Liouville again). So a single scalar $G$ manufactures a whole one-parameter family of area-preserving maps. Three choices of $G$ give the three basic moves every symplectic map is built from — pick a generator, drag $t$, and watch the blue patch transported. The dashed patch is the starting position; areas are equal at every $t$.

Key insight: A Lie transformation $e^{t:G:}$ turns any generator $G$ into a symplectic map by flowing along $X_G$. Rotation, shear, and squeeze — the harmonic oscillator, the drift, and the hyperbolic saddle — are just three generators run through the same exponential.

8.3 — Kick, then drift: assembling a real map

The maps we actually care about are compositions of these elementary flows. A charged particle drifts between magnets, then gets a sharp nonlinear kick at a magnet; a kicked rotor drifts in angle, then feels an impulse. In Lie language a "drift then kick" map factorises as

$$T = e^{:K(q):}\, e^{:\,\frac{1}{2}p^2:},$$

a kick generated by a potential $K(q)$ following a free drift generated by $\tfrac12 p^2$. This is exactly the origin of the standard map of Module 6 (drift in angle, sinusoidal kick) and, with a polynomial kick, of the Hénon map of Module 9. Splitting a map into exponentials like this is also how symplectic integrators work: they approximate a hard flow by a product of easy ones, and — crucially — the product is exactly area-preserving even when it is only approximately the right dynamics.

Key insight: Complicated symplectic maps are products of simple Lie transforms — drifts and kicks. Composing exponentials, not adding Hamiltonians, is the discrete-time grammar of mechanics.

8.4 — Why order matters: non-commutation is the bracket

Here is the subtlety that makes the whole subject nontrivial. For ordinary numbers $e^A e^B = e^{A+B}$. For Lie transforms it is false unless the generators commute. The Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff formula gives the correction, and the leading correction is precisely the Lie bracket:

$$e^{:A:}\,e^{:B:} = \exp\!\Big(:A: + :B: + \tfrac{1}{2}:\{A,B\}: + \cdots\Big).$$

So $\{A, B\}$ measures the failure of two flows to commute. We can see it: run generator $A$ forward, then $B$ forward, then $A$ backward, then $B$ backward. If the flows commuted you would return exactly to the start. The gap that remains is the commutator, and for small step $s$ it scales like $s^2\{A,B\}$. Drag the step size, and toggle between a commuting pair and a non-commuting pair.

Key insight: The Poisson bracket is the infinitesimal gap between doing things in different orders. A vanishing bracket means the two motions are compatible — the same conserved-quantity-in-involution condition that made systems integrable in Module 6.

8.5 — Straightening the motion: normal forms & the twist

Now the payoff. Given a nonlinear map, the normal form program uses a sequence of near-identity Lie transforms $e^{:G_1:}, e^{:G_2:}, \dots$ — each $G_n$ chosen to kill the angle-dependent part at order $n$ — until, in the new coordinates, the map depends only on the action $J$:

$$\mathcal H[J] = \nu_0\,J + \frac{\tau_0}{2!}J^2 + \frac{\tau_1}{3!}J^3 + \cdots$$

All the hard nonlinearity has been absorbed into choosing coordinates, exactly as action–angle variables did for flows in Module 6 — but now done order-by-order, algebraically, with brackets. The reward is the amplitude dependence of the tune, obtained by differentiating:

$$\nu(J) = \frac{\partial \mathcal H}{\partial J} = \nu_0 + \tau_0\,J + \tau_1\,\frac{J^2}{2} + \cdots$$

The number $\tau_0$ is the twist (or first detuning coefficient): how the rotation number drifts as the orbit gets bigger. We can measure it without doing any of the algebra — just iterate the cubic Hénon map $q'=p,\ p'=-q+p+p^3$ (Module 9's star example) at many amplitudes and read off how fast each orbit rotates. The measured points fall off a straight line: near the centre the twist $\tau_0 \approx -0.16$ captures the detuning beautifully, but as $J$ grows the curve bends — the higher coefficients $\tau_1, \tau_2, \dots$ are waking up, and eventually the whole expansion will fail near a resonance. Computing $\tau_0, \tau_1, \dots$ to high order by hand is exactly what the Lie-algebra method automates, and it is the benchmark the target paper measures its own method against.

The catch that motivates Module 10. The normal-form series is gorgeous but local: it lives in an infinitesimal neighbourhood of the fixed point, it says nothing beyond the separatrix, and its denominators explode whenever the tune passes through a rational — the small-denominator problem at the heart of KAM. To describe the global geometry — islands, the separatrix, the edge of chaos — we need a complementary idea: not a perfect invariant in perfect coordinates, but a function conserved almost exactly across the whole plane. That is the subject of the final module, and of the paper it builds toward.
