The Legendre Transform

Module 1 left us holding a clue: the quantity $p = \partial L/\partial\dot x$ kept appearing, and it was nothing but the momentum. The Euler–Lagrange equation is one second-order equation in the variables $(x, \dot x)$. What happens if we refuse to treat velocity $\dot x$ as the fundamental partner of position, and instead promote $p$ to a coordinate of equal standing?

To do that we need a reversible way to swap a variable for the slope of a function in that variable. That tool is the Legendre transform, and it is worth meeting on its own before we unleash it on mechanics.

2.1 — Encoding a curve by its tangent lines

A convex function $f(v)$ can be described two equivalent ways. The obvious way is the graph: for each $v$, the height $f(v)$. The second way is to forget the points and record only the tangent lines — for each slope $p$, the line of that slope that just touches the curve from below. A convex curve is the envelope of its tangents, so no information is lost. The Legendre transform is the dictionary between these two descriptions.

Concretely: a line of slope $p$ has the form $y = pv - c$. To make it a supporting tangent we push it up (increase $c$ is down, so we lower $c$) until it just kisses the curve. The required intercept is

$$f^\star(p) = \max_v\big(\,p v - f(v)\,\big),$$

and $-f^\star(p)$ is exactly the $y$-intercept of that tangent line. Below, $f(v) = \tfrac12 m v^2$ is the kinetic energy as a function of velocity. Drag the slope $p$ to slide the tangent line along the curve; the right panel builds the transform $f^\star(p) = H(p)$ point by point. Drag $m$ to change the curvature.

Two facts jump out of the picture. First, the contact point sits at the velocity $v^\star$ where the curve's own slope equals $p$, i.e. $f'(v^\star) = p$ — for kinetic energy that is $mv^\star = p$, the familiar $p = mv$. Second, watch the curvatures: making $f$ steeper (larger $m$) makes its transform $H$ flatter. The transform inverts curvature, $H''(p) = 1/f''(v) = 1/m$. A stiff function has a soft dual.

Key insight: The Legendre transform re-expresses a convex function in terms of its slope instead of its argument. Convexity (a single tangent per slope) is exactly what makes it reversible: transforming twice returns the original, $f^{\star\star} = f$.

2.2 — From the Lagrangian to the Hamiltonian

In one line: the Hamiltonian is the Legendre transform of the Lagrangian in $\dot x$. The rest of this section unpacks what that means.

Now apply this to mechanics. We transform $L$ in the velocity $\dot x$ while position $x$ rides along as a spectator. The slope variable here — the new independent coordinate that will replace velocity — is the momentum $p$. By the definition from 2.1, the transform is

$$H(x, p) \;=\; \max_{\dot x}\big(\,p\,\dot x - L(x, \dot x)\,\big).$$

Read this carefully: $p$ is held fixed, and we maximize over $\dot x$. To find the maximizing velocity, differentiate the bracket in $\dot x$ and set it to zero:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \dot x}\big(p\,\dot x - L\big) = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x} = p.$$

This equation ties together the fixed slope $p$ and the velocity that achieves the max: the maximizer is the velocity at which $L$'s own slope equals $p$. Because $L$ is convex in $\dot x$ (see the note below), there is exactly one such velocity, and the equation can be inverted to give it explicitly as a function of $p$, written $\dot x(p)$. Solving $\partial L/\partial\dot x = p$ for $\dot x$ is the construction of $\dot x(p)$.

The relation $\partial L/\partial\dot x = p$ is the same momentum that kept reappearing in Module 1; here it does double duty, both defining the momentum $p$ from a velocity and, read backwards, recovering the velocity $\dot x(p)$ from the momentum. Substituting the maximizer back into the bracket gives the Hamiltonian:

$$H(x, p) \;=\; p\,\dot x(p) \;-\; L\big(x,\, \dot x(p)\big).$$

Every $\dot x$ on the right has been replaced by $\dot x(p)$, so $H$ is genuinely a function of position and momentum, never velocity. For our particle, $\partial L/\partial\dot x = m\dot x = p$ gives $\dot x(p) = p/m$, hence

$$H = p\cdot\frac{p}{m} - \left(\tfrac12 m\frac{p^2}{m^2} - V\right) = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V(x) = T + V.$$

The minus sign of the Lagrangian has flipped into a plus: $H$ is the total energy, kinetic plus potential. (This identification $H = T+V$ holds whenever the kinetic energy is a quadratic form in the velocities and the constraints do not depend on time — true for everything in this course.)

But is $L$ convex? Section 2.1 warned that the transform is only reversible for a convex function — yet we never checked $L$. We don't have to check it in $x$: the transform acts only in $\dot x$, with $x$ a spectator, so a wildly non-convex potential $V(x)$ is irrelevant. We need convexity in $\dot x$ alone, and the kinetic term hands it to us for free:
$$\frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial \dot x^2} = m > 0.$$
A positive mass makes $L$ strictly convex in $\dot x$ (in several dimensions: the mass matrix is positive-definite). That is exactly what lets us solve $p = m\dot x$ for $\dot x = p/m$ uniquely above — one velocity per momentum, the mechanical echo of "one tangent per slope." Strictly, all we need is that the velocity Hessian $\partial^2 L/\partial\dot x^2$ be non-degenerate; positive-definiteness is the clean, global version, and it holds automatically for the quadratic kinetic energies in this course.
$L$ and $H$ are Legendre duals. Section 2.1 showed the transform is an involution: do it twice and you return home, $f^{\star\star}=f$. The same holds here. Transform $L$ in $\dot x$ and you get $H(x,p)$; transform $H$ in $p$ and you land back on $L(x,\dot x)$, losing nothing. Neither object is more fundamental — they are a dual pair carrying identical information, one indexed by velocity, the other by momentum.

The duality is symmetric in the conjugate slot but twisted in the spectator. The velocity–momentum pairing has no sign: $p = \partial L/\partial\dot x$ going out, $\dot x = \partial H/\partial p$ coming back (the latter is Hamilton's first equation, next section). But position picks up a minus, $\partial H/\partial x = -\partial L/\partial x$. The reason is purely structural: in $H = p\,\dot x(p) - L$, the only term that carries $x$ is $-L$. The pairing term $p\,\dot x$ holds no explicit position, and the $x$-dependence hiding inside $\dot x(p)$ contributes nothing — at the maximizing velocity the slope in $\dot x$ is zero, so nudging it costs nothing to first order. Differentiating in $x$ therefore leaves only $-\partial L/\partial x$. The minus is inherited straight from the minus on $L$ in the transform. In 2.3 this same sign reappears as $\dot p = -\partial H/\partial x$.

2.3 — Hamilton's equations, in one line of algebra

Here is the payoff. Take the differential of $H = p\dot x - L$, letting all of $x$, $\dot x$, $p$ vary:

$$dH = \dot x\,dp + p\,d\dot x - \frac{\partial L}{\partial x}dx - \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x}d\dot x.$$

The middle terms cancel by the very definition $p = \partial L/\partial\dot x$: $\;p\,d\dot x - (\partial L/\partial\dot x)\,d\dot x = 0$. This cancellation is the magic of the Legendre transform — the velocity differential vanishes, confirming $H$ truly depends only on $x$ and $p$. What survives is

$$dH = \dot x\,dp - \frac{\partial L}{\partial x}\,dx.$$

But $dH = \frac{\partial H}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}dp$ identically. Matching the coefficients of the independent differentials $dx$ and $dp$:

$$\frac{\partial H}{\partial p} = \dot x, \qquad \frac{\partial H}{\partial x} = -\frac{\partial L}{\partial x}.$$

The second one needs the Euler–Lagrange equation to finish. Recall $\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot x} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial x}$, and the left side is just $\dot p$. So $\partial L/\partial x = \dot p$, and therefore $\partial H/\partial x = -\dot p$. Collecting both results gives Hamilton's canonical equations:

$$\boxed{\;\dot x = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}, \qquad \dot p = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial x}\;}$$

For $n$ degrees of freedom there is one such pair per coordinate, $\dot q_i = \partial H/\partial p_i$ and $\dot p_i = -\partial H/\partial q_i$. We have traded one second-order equation for two first-order equations — and they are almost mirror images of each other, differing only by a single minus sign. That near-symmetry is not cosmetic; it is the seed of every conservation law and geometric structure in the rest of the course.

Check on the oscillator: For a spring, $H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac12 kx^2$. Then $\dot x = \partial H/\partial p = p/m$ and $\dot p = -\partial H/\partial x = -kx$. Differentiate the first and substitute the second: $m\ddot x = \dot p = -kx$ — simple harmonic motion, recovered with no second derivatives anywhere in the formulation.

2.4 — Reading the equations

The two equations say something physical and worth pausing on:

  • $\dot x = \partial H/\partial p$ — how position changes is dictated by how the energy depends on momentum. For $H = p^2/2m + V$ this is just $\dot x = p/m$, the definition of momentum read backwards.
  • $\dot p = -\partial H/\partial x$ — momentum flows downhill in the potential. The minus sign means the force $-\partial V/\partial x$ pushes toward lower energy.

Notice that $x$ and $p$ now sit on completely equal footing — both are just coordinates evolving in time, governed by partial derivatives of one master function $H$. There is no longer a privileged "position space"; there is a phase space with axes $x$ and $p$, and the state of the system is a single point in it.

2.5 — The thread to pull next

We have a point $(x, p)$ and a rule, $(\dot x, \dot p) = (\partial H/\partial p,\, -\partial H/\partial x)$, that tells it where to go next. That is precisely the data of a vector field: at every point of the plane, an arrow. The trajectory of the system is just the streamline you get by following the arrows.

So mechanics becomes the study of a flow on a plane. What do these flows look like? Why can trajectories never cross? And what curve does a given orbit trace? Module 3 moves into phase space and watches the flow.
