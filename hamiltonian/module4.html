Poisson Brackets & Conservation

Module 3 ended on a question: besides the energy $H$, what other quantities stay constant as a system evolves — and can we find them without inspired guessing? The answer is a single operation that computes the rate of change of any phase-space quantity along the flow, and reduces "is this conserved?" to checking whether one expression equals zero.

4.1 — The rate of change of anything

Let $f(q, p)$ be any function on phase space — energy, momentum, angular momentum, the $x$-coordinate, whatever. As the state $(q(t), p(t))$ flows, $f$ rides along and its value changes. By the chain rule,

$$\frac{df}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial q}\dot q + \frac{\partial f}{\partial p}\dot p.$$

Now substitute Hamilton's equations $\dot q = \partial H/\partial p$ and $\dot p = -\partial H/\partial q$. The velocities disappear, leaving a formula built entirely from derivatives of $f$ and $H$:

$$\frac{df}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial q}\frac{\partial H}{\partial p} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial p}\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}.$$

This combination appears so often it gets a name and a notation. The Poisson bracket of two phase-space functions $f$ and $g$ is

$$\{f, g\} \;\equiv\; \sum_i\left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial q_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial p_i} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial q_i} \right).$$

With it, the evolution of every observable collapses to one elegant line (adding $\partial f/\partial t$ only if $f$ depends on time explicitly):

$$\boxed{\;\frac{df}{dt} = \{f, H\} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}\;}$$
Key insight: The Hamiltonian doesn't just generate the motion of $q$ and $p$ — through the bracket $\{\,\cdot\,, H\}$ it generates the motion of every quantity at once. A function is conserved precisely when $\{f, H\} = 0$.

4.2 — Energy, for free, again

Test the formula on $f = H$ itself. The bracket of anything with itself vanishes by antisymmetry — every term is subtracted from its twin:

$$\frac{dH}{dt} = \{H, H\} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial q}\frac{\partial H}{\partial p} - \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}\frac{\partial H}{\partial q} = 0.$$

Energy conservation drops out in one symbol. The bracket also encodes the canonical structure itself: position and its conjugate momentum satisfy

$$\{q, p\} = \frac{\partial q}{\partial q}\frac{\partial p}{\partial p} - \frac{\partial q}{\partial p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial q} = 1, \qquad \{q, q\} = \{p, p\} = 0.$$

These fundamental brackets are the fingerprint of a canonical coordinate pair. (Anyone who has seen quantum mechanics will recognise $\{q,p\}=1$ as the classical shadow of the commutator $[\hat q, \hat p] = i\hbar$ — the bracket is replaced by $\tfrac1{i\hbar}[\,\cdot\,,\cdot\,]$. We return to this at the very end of the course.)

4.3 — Symmetry ⟺ conservation (Noether, computationally)

The bracket is antisymmetric: $\{f, H\} = -\{H, f\}$. So the statement "$f$ is conserved by the flow of $H$" ($\{f,H\}=0$) is identical to "$H$ is unchanged by the flow generated by $f$" ($\{H,f\}=0$). Read the second way, it is a statement about symmetry. This is Noether's theorem stripped to its algebraic core:

  • Translation symmetry → momentum. The function $p$ generates spatial translations $q \mapsto q + \varepsilon$ (since $\{q, p\} = 1$). If $H$ doesn't depend on $q$, then $\{p, H\} = -\partial H/\partial q = 0$, so momentum is conserved.
  • Rotational symmetry → angular momentum. In two dimensions $L = q_x p_y - q_y p_x$ generates rotations. If $H$ depends only on the radius $r = \sqrt{q_x^2 + q_y^2}$ — a central force — then $\{L, H\} = 0$ and $L$ is conserved.
  • Time-translation symmetry → energy. If $H$ has no explicit time dependence, $\partial H/\partial t = 0$, and $dH/dt = 0$.

The demo below is a planet in a central gravitational field, $H = \tfrac12(p_x^2 + p_y^2) - 1/r$. The potential cares only about distance, so it is rotationally symmetric but not translationally symmetric. The theory therefore predicts: energy $H$ conserved, angular momentum $L$ conserved, but the linear momentum component $p_x$ not conserved. Verify $\{L, H\} = 0$ by hand once and the picture stops being a coincidence. With $L = xp_y - yp_x$ and $H = \tfrac12(p_x^2+p_y^2) + V(r)$, the bracket expands to $\{L,H\} = x\,\dot p_y\!\big|_{\text{rot}} + \dots$; collecting terms, the kinetic part contributes $p_xp_y - p_yp_x = 0$, and the potential part contributes $-\big(x\,\partial_y - y\,\partial_x\big)V(r) = 0$ because $V$ depends only on $r$. Both halves vanish — exactly the rotational symmetry, now a closed calculation rather than a hope.

4.4 — The bracket has its own algebra

The Poisson bracket is not just notation; it makes phase-space functions into a Lie algebra, obeying

  • Antisymmetry: $\{f, g\} = -\{g, f\}$.
  • Bilinearity and the Leibniz rule $\{f, gh\} = \{f, g\}h + g\{f, h\}$ — it differentiates products like a derivative should.
  • The Jacobi identity: $\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\} = 0$.

The Jacobi identity has a beautiful payoff: if $f$ and $g$ are both conserved ($\{f,H\}=\{g,H\}=0$), then so is their bracket $\{f, g\}$. Conserved quantities are closed under the bracket — a machine for manufacturing new constants of motion from old ones. (Sometimes the new one is trivial, but for systems like the symmetric top it produces genuinely independent conservation laws.)

The same algebra runs quantum mechanics. Those three laws — antisymmetry, bilinearity, Jacobi — are the definition of a Lie algebra, and they are obeyed by one other operation you may have met: the commutator $[\hat A, \hat B] = \hat A\hat B - \hat B\hat A$ of quantum operators. It too is antisymmetric, bilinear, a derivation, and Jacobi. So the classical observables under $\{\,\cdot\,,\cdot\,\}$ and the quantum operators under $[\,\cdot\,,\cdot\,]$ are the same kind of object, and quantization is the bridge between them:
$$\{f, g\} \;\longmapsto\; \frac{1}{i\hbar}\,[\hat f, \hat g].$$
This is a Lie-algebra homomorphism (exact to leading order in $\hbar$). Everything in this module has a mirror image across it. The canonical bracket $\{q,p\}=1$ becomes the canonical commutation relation $[\hat q, \hat p] = i\hbar$. The evolution law $\dot f = \{f, H\}$ of 4.1 becomes Heisenberg's equation $\dot{\hat A} = \tfrac1{i\hbar}[\hat A, \hat H]$. And "$f$ is conserved" $\Leftrightarrow \{f,H\}=0$ becomes "$\hat A$ commutes with $\hat H$" $\Leftrightarrow [\hat A,\hat H]=0$ — the quantum statement that an observable is a constant of motion. The vanishing-bracket test for conservation is the same idea in both worlds.

4.5 — The thread to pull next

We can now decide whether any single quantity is conserved. But the bracket structure controls more than individual functions — it constrains the geometry of the flow as a whole. The fundamental relation $\{q, p\} = 1$ is secretly a statement about area: as the flow carries a patch of phase space around, that bracket forces the patch's area to stay fixed, no matter how violently it is stretched and folded.

That is Liouville's theorem, and it has a startling consequence — Hamiltonian systems can never have attractors. Module 5.
