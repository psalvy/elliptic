Action–Angle by Hand

Module 6 stated the punchline — integrable motion is "just rigid rotation in disguise," uniform winding of an angle on a torus — and then rushed on to chaos. If that felt like a magic trick, this interlude slows it right down and does the trick twice, by hand, in one degree of freedom, where a "torus" is nothing scarier than a closed orbit. By the end the phrase action–angle variables should feel concrete, and you will have met — in the flesh — the single idea that the whole map half of the course (Modules 7–10) is built on.

6.1.1 — What we are actually trying to do

A one-degree-of-freedom system has a 2D phase space $(q,p)$ and, if it is bound, its orbits are closed loops — the level sets of the energy $H(q,p)=E$. The action–angle idea is a change of coordinates $(q,p)\to(J,\theta)$ chosen so that the messy motion along a loop becomes the simplest motion imaginable:

$$\dot J = 0 \quad(\text{stay on your loop}), \qquad \dot\theta = \omega(J)=\text{const} \quad(\text{wind at a steady rate}).$$

Two questions have to be answered to make this real: which number labels the loop (that is $J$), and how fast does the angle wind (that is $\omega$). Here are the answers.

  • The action is the area the loop encloses, divided by $2\pi$:
    $$J = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint p\,dq = \frac{\text{area enclosed by the orbit}}{2\pi}.$$
    It is constant along the motion for the same reason Liouville's theorem holds — the loop is fixed, so the area it bounds is fixed.
  • The frequency is how the energy changes as you inflate the loop:
    $$\omega(J) = \frac{dH}{dJ}.$$

Let's watch both come out of two concrete systems.

Isn't $(q,p)$ already an action–angle pair?

A fair objection: $q$ and $p$ are also a conjugate pair, $\{q,p\}=1$, so why isn't $q$ "the action" and $p$ "the angle"? Because action–angle is not just any relabelling of the plane. Two conditions make it special, and they are the whole content:

  • The change must be canonical — it must preserve the bracket, $\{\theta, J\} = 1$, so Hamilton's equations keep their form. That already excludes almost every coordinate change.
  • The Hamiltonian must end up depending on $J$ alone, $H = H(J)$ with no $\theta$ in it. This is the non-trivial demand — and finding coordinates that pull it off is exactly what it means to have solved the system.

Everything else follows from the second condition: $\dot J = -\partial H/\partial\theta = 0$ precisely because $H$ has no $\theta$, and $\dot\theta = \partial H/\partial J$ is then a constant. So the two variables are not interchangeable — one is conserved, the other winds uniformly. Check it against the oscillator below: along any orbit both $q$ and $p$ oscillate — neither is conserved — while $J = E$ sits still, and $H$ depends on $q$ and $p$ together but on $J$ by itself. That asymmetry is what earns the names "action" and "angle"; raw $(q,p)$ has neither property.

Key insight: Action–angle variables are a canonical change of coordinates picked so that $H$ sees only $J$ — which is the same thing as finding a conserved quantity. It is generally nonlinear, exists only locally (inside the separatrix), and for a chaotic system does not exist at all. $(q,p)$ is a conjugate pair, but not an action–angle pair, because neither $q$ nor $p$ is conserved.

6.1.2 — The harmonic oscillator: the loop is a circle

Take $H = \tfrac12(p^2 + q^2)$. Its level sets $p^2+q^2 = 2E$ are circles of radius $\sqrt{2E}$. The enclosed area is $\pi(\sqrt{2E})^2 = 2\pi E$, so the action is simply

$$J = \frac{2\pi E}{2\pi} = E, \qquad\text{and}\qquad \omega = \frac{dH}{dJ} = \frac{dE}{dE} = 1.$$

The change of variables is an exact polar substitution — $q = \sqrt{2J}\cos\theta$, $p = -\sqrt{2J}\sin\theta$ — and plugging in confirms $H = J$ and $\dot\theta = 1$. Drag the energy: the dot rides the circle at a constant angular speed no matter how big the loop is. The shaded disk is the area whose value (over $2\pi$) is $J$.

Key insight: For the harmonic oscillator the action is the energy and the frequency is the same on every orbit. It is the one system whose frequency does not care about amplitude — which is exactly why it is the boring "zeroth-order" case in everything that follows.

6.1.3 — The pendulum: frequency that remembers how hard you pushed

Now let the restoring force bend: $H = \tfrac12 p^2 + (1-\cos q)$. Small swings look like the oscillator (circles), but larger swings fatten and distort, and at $E=2$ the orbit stretches into the separatrix — the pendulum balancing straight up. The action is still the enclosed area over $2\pi$, but now the loops are not circles and the integral must be done numerically. The consequence is the whole point of this interlude: the frequency drops as the amplitude grows. A big swing takes longer.

Drag the amplitude. On the left the dot rides the real orbit — watch it speed up at the bottom and dawdle near the turning points, motion that is anything but uniform in the raw $(q,p)$ coordinates. So the angle variable $\theta$ does not measure the geometric polar angle of the dot — that would run fast at the bottom and slow at the top. It is defined precisely so that it advances uniformly, $\theta = \omega t$, absorbing all the non-uniformity into the choice of coordinate. That is the magic of Module 6 made explicit: you trade a hard motion for a trivial one by measuring angle with the right clock.

Is $\theta$ just rescaled time, then?

Almost — and it is worth being exact about the "almost." Follow one orbit and $\theta = \omega t$ is literally the time elapsed since you passed $\theta=0$, rescaled so that the orbit's full period $T = 2\pi/\omega$ counts as $2\pi$. On a single trajectory, $\theta$ is the clock reading. Two things stop it from being merely a relabelling of time:

  • $\theta$ is a coordinate on the plane, not on the time axis. It is a function $\theta(q,p)$ defined at every point of the region filled by loops — you can read it off a point without running the dynamics. It says where on the loop you are, not when you got there. The two only coincide along a trajectory.
  • Every orbit needs its own clock speed. A genuine reparametrisation of time is one global rescaling of $t$. But each loop winds at its own $\omega(J)$, so no single rescaling straightens them all at once — you divide each orbit's time-of-flight by that loop's period. That per-orbit normalisation is exactly what promotes "time" into a phase-space coordinate. (The harmonic oscillator, with $\omega=1$ on every orbit, is the degenerate case where $\theta$ really is just $t$ everywhere — which is why it looks too easy.)

Put together: $\theta$ is time-of-flight rescaled so each orbit's period is $2\pi$, and $J$ says which orbit — which clock. And unlike a time relabelling, the pair is canonical, $\{\theta,J\}=1$, with $H$ depending on $J$ alone. When we cross to maps, this becomes discrete: one iterate advances $\theta$ by a fixed tick $2\pi\,\nu(J)$, so counting turns $n$ plays the role of time and $\theta \approx 2\pi\nu\, n$.

Key insight: For any system except the harmonic oscillator, the winding frequency $\omega$ depends on which loop you are on — that is, on the action $J$. Bigger orbits generally wind slower. The number $d\omega/dJ$ — how quickly the frequency drifts with amplitude — is the system's twist.

6.1.4 — Why this is the doorway to Modules 7–10

Everything above was for a flow in continuous time. When we slice a flow with a Poincaré section (Module 6) or study a system that is naturally discrete — a kicked rotor, a beam getting one magnet kick per turn — the flow becomes a map, and each of today's objects has an exact twin waiting in the next four modules:

$$ \begin{array}{ccc} \textbf{Flow (this interlude)} & \longleftrightarrow & \textbf{Map (Modules 7–10)}\\[4pt] \text{action } J & \longleftrightarrow & \text{action } J \text{ (labels the invariant loop)}\\[2pt] \text{frequency } \omega(J) & \longleftrightarrow & \text{rotation number } \nu(J)\\[2pt] \text{twist } d\omega/dJ & \longleftrightarrow & \text{twist coefficient } \tau_0\\[2pt] \text{closed orbit } H=E & \longleftrightarrow & \text{invariant curve } \mathcal K=\text{const} \end{array} $$

When Module 8 says the normal form absorbs the nonlinearity into coordinates "exactly as action–angle variables did for flows," this is what it means: the map's rotation number $\nu(J)$ that varies with amplitude (Module 7 will also call it the tune) is the discrete echo of the pendulum's $\omega(J)$ you just dragged, and the twist $\tau_0$ is the discrete echo of the falling orange curve's slope. You have now built the intuition the rest of the course leans on. Onward to maps.
