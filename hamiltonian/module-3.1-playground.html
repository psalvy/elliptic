The Phase-Space Playground

No new theory here — just room to play. You have met the action, the Legendre transform, Hamilton's equations, and the idea that orbits ride the level sets of $H$. This sandbox lets you steer all of it at once. Pick a Hamiltonian, then click anywhere to drop a state and watch it flow.

Each preset is a separable Hamiltonian $H(q,p) = \tfrac12 p^2 + V(q)$, so the kinetic part is always $\tfrac12 p^2$ and only the potential changes. The flow is

$$\dot q = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p} = p, \qquad \dot p = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q} = -V'(q).$$

Things to try. On the double well, launch just inside one lobe of the figure-eight, then just outside — the figure-eight separatrix divides "trapped in one well" from "sloshing over both." On any system, switch to drop a blob and watch a little cloud of states get sheared and stretched as it flows — yet its area never changes. That conservation of phase-space area is Liouville's theorem, and it is the subject of Module 5. Notice the blob can become a long thin filament but never shrinks to nothing or piles up.

The potentials

  • Pendulum: $V(q) = k\,(1-\cos q)$ — one well per $2\pi$, centres at the bottom, saddles at the top.
  • Harmonic: $V(q) = \tfrac12 k\,q^2$ — a single parabolic well; every orbit is an ellipse and the period is independent of amplitude.
  • Double well: $V(q) = \tfrac14 q^4 - \tfrac12 k\,q^2$ — two stable minima separated by an unstable hilltop at the origin; the home of the figure-eight separatrix.
  • Quartic: $V(q) = \tfrac14 k\,q^4$ — a single soft well; unlike the harmonic case the period shrinks as the amplitude grows (a nonlinear oscillator).
