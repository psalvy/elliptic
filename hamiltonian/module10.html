Almost-Conserved Quantities

This is where the course meets the frontier. The cubic Hénon map has no exact invariant — chaos forbids it. So we stop demanding perfection and ask for something weaker but astonishingly useful: a function the map conserves almost exactly, whose level sets trace the real orbits. We build it from the Courant–Snyder ellipse of Module 7, correct it order by order, and watch it snap onto the tangle from Module 9. This is precisely the construction of the paper this course was written to unlock.

10.1 — Relaxing the impossible: the residual

An exact invariant satisfies $\mathcal K[T\zeta] - \mathcal K[\zeta] = 0$ at every point. For a chaotic map no such smooth $\mathcal K$ exists. Introduce a small parameter $\epsilon$ measuring the amplitude of the motion (rescale $(q,p)\to\epsilon(q,p)$: near the fixed point the map is nearly linear, and $\epsilon$ controls how much the nonlinearity bites). Then ask only that $\mathcal K$ be conserved to some order in $\epsilon$:

$$\mathcal R_n \;\equiv\; \mathcal K^{(n)}[T\zeta] - \mathcal K^{(n)}[\zeta] \;=\; \mathcal O(\epsilon^{\,n+1}).$$

$\mathcal R_n$ is the residual — the direct, measurable amount by which the "invariant" fails after one step. An exact invariant has $\mathcal R = 0$; a good approximate one has a residual so small that orbits drift across level sets only over astronomically many iterations. The residual is something we can literally compute at any point, and it is our scoreboard for the rest of the module.

Key insight: Give up on $\mathcal R = 0$ and ask for $\mathcal R = \mathcal O(\epsilon^{n+1})$. "Almost conserved to high order" is enough to pin orbits down for practical eternities — and it exists where exact invariants cannot.

10.2 — Correcting the ellipse, order by order

Expand the invariant as a power series in $\epsilon$, each term a polynomial in $(q,p)$:

$$\mathcal K^{(n)} = \mathcal K_0 + \epsilon\,\mathcal K_1 + \epsilon^2\mathcal K_2 + \cdots + \epsilon^n\mathcal K_n.$$

The zeroth order is forced by the linearised map — it is the Courant–Snyder invariant of Module 7 (with $a = f'(0) = 1$):

$$\mathcal K_0 = \mathrm{C.S.} = p^2 - p\,q + q^2.$$

Demanding $\mathcal R_n = \mathcal O(\epsilon^{n+1})$ at each order fixes the higher polynomials. Carrying this out for the cubic Hénon map gives the second-order approximate invariant (this is Eq. K4.a of the paper):

$$\mathcal K^{(2)}_{4.a} = p^2 - p\,q + q^2 - p^2 q^2.$$

Key insight: The order-0 invariant is just the harmonic-oscillator ellipse; each higher order adds a polynomial correction that bends the level sets to follow the real, nonlinear orbits. The residual drops by a full power of the amplitude at every order.

10.3 — The useful ambiguity: refining the separatrix

Module 7 warned us that any function of an invariant is again an invariant — a freedom encoded by free coefficients $C_i$. It does not change the residual order, but it reshapes the level sets. The paper exploits it to make the invariant hug the separatrix — the boundary curve through the saddles at $(\pm1,\pm1)$. Adding the allowed term $\tfrac{7}{5}\,\mathrm{C.S.}^2$ gives the refined invariant K4.b:

$$\mathcal K^{(2)}_{4.b} = p^2 - p\,q + q^2 - p^2 q^2 + \tfrac{7}{5}\big(p^2 - p\,q + q^2\big)^2.$$

Both K4.a and K4.b are second order — same residual scaling — yet K4.b's level set through the saddle sits almost exactly on the true separatrix, giving the best global picture of where stable motion ends. Select K₄ᵦ above and compare its outermost curve to K₄ₐ: same accuracy near the centre, better geometry at the edge. Low order, chosen well, captures the qualitative truth.

10.4 — Where the series bites its own tail: small denominators

Why not just push to order 50 and conserve everything? Because at each order the correction is obtained by dividing by a factor that vanishes whenever the tune is rational. Schematically the $n$-th correction carries denominators like

$$\frac{1}{\,e^{\,2\pi i\,m\,\nu} - 1\,}, \qquad \nu = \frac{m'}{m}\ \Rightarrow\ \text{denominator} \to 0.$$

These are the small denominators at the heart of KAM theory (Module 6). A rational tune $\nu = m'/m$ is a resonance: the orbit closes after $m$ steps and locks, forming the island chains we saw in Module 9. There the naive series blows up, and the flat detuning curve $\nu(J)$ of Module 8 develops the mode-locked plateaus (Arnold tongues) that no polynomial invariant can follow. The paper's answer is an averaging procedure that turns the expansion into a resonant theory: near a rational tune it integrates powers of trigonometric functions instead of dividing by zero, producing invariants valid right through the low-order resonances — islands and all.

Key insight: The perturbation series is an asymptotic, not convergent, expansion: it improves then diverges, defeated by resonances. Handling them — not ignoring them — is what separates a toy calculation from a theory that describes real island chains.

10.5 — You are ready to read the paper

Everything above is the ground floor of

T. Zolkin, S. Nagaitsev, I. Morozov, S. Kladov, “Geometry of Almost-Conserved Quantities in Symplectic Maps. Part I: Perturbation Theory,” arXiv:2505.07223 (2025).

Its logic, now fully in reach: it builds approximate invariants $\mathcal K^{(n)}$ for reversible symplectic maps in McMillan form (Module 9), takes the Courant–Snyder invariant as zeroth order (Module 7), fixes higher orders by killing the residual (§10.2), benchmarks the resulting twist coefficients against the Lie-algebra method (Module 8), and introduces the averaging / resonant theory to survive small denominators (§10.4). The organising analogy is Noether-for-maps: continuous symmetry gives exact invariants, reversibility gives approximate ones (Module 9).
