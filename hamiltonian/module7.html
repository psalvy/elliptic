From Flow to Map

In Module 6 we sliced a continuous flow with a plane and marked a dot at each crossing. A smooth trajectory in time became a sequence of jumps — a map $T$ that sends the plane to itself, $\zeta \mapsto \zeta' = T[\zeta]$. That was a convenience for drawing. This module takes it seriously: the discrete map is a full-blooded dynamical system with its own rules, and almost everything we built for flows — conservation, stability, tori, the rotation of angles — has a sharper, more visible discrete twin. This is the world the frontier lives in.

Why bother leaving flows behind? A particle circulating a storage ring receives a kick from the same magnets on every turn; a periodically driven oscillator feels the same push each period. Nature itself hands us the strobe. The natural object is not "where is the state at time $t$" but "where is it after one more turn" — a map.

7.1 — A map that never loses area

The simplest nonlinear-friendly map we can write keeps momentum and position on equal footing. Read the old momentum off as the new position, and reflect the old position with a linear kick:

$$T:\qquad q' = p, \qquad p' = -q + a\,p.$$

One number $a$ controls everything. To iterate is to apply $T$ again and again, tracing an orbit $\zeta_0 \to \zeta_1 \to \zeta_2 \to \cdots$. Just as Liouville's theorem (Module 5) said the continuous flow never compresses phase space, a map earns the name symplectic (in the plane: area-preserving) when its Jacobian has unit determinant:

$$\mathrm{d}T = \begin{bmatrix} \partial q'/\partial q & \partial q'/\partial p \\[2pt] \partial p'/\partial q & \partial p'/\partial p \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & a \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \det \mathrm{d}T = (0)(a) - (1)(-1) = 1.$$

The determinant is the local area-scaling factor, and here it is exactly $1$ for every value of $a$ — Liouville, made discrete. Drag $a$ and watch the little blue tile get carried around by the map: it shears, rotates, stretches — but its area never changes.

Key insight: A symplectic map is the discrete face of Liouville's theorem — $\det \mathrm{d}T = 1$ means every application shuffles phase space without ever squeezing or expanding its area.

7.2 — Stable or doomed: the trace decides

Because the map is linear, an orbit is completely governed by the eigenvalues of $\mathrm{d}T$. For a symplectic $2\times 2$ matrix $\det = 1$, so the eigenvalues multiply to one: $\lambda_1 \lambda_2 = 1$. The characteristic equation is

$$\lambda^2 - (\operatorname{tr}\mathrm{d}T)\,\lambda + 1 = 0, \qquad \operatorname{tr}\mathrm{d}T = a.$$

Everything hinges on the single quantity $a = \operatorname{tr}\mathrm{d}T$:

  • $|a| < 2$ — the roots are complex conjugates on the unit circle, $\lambda = e^{\pm i\mu}$. Neither grows. The fixed point at the origin is elliptic (stable): orbits circulate on closed loops.
  • $|a| > 2$ — the roots are real, $\lambda$ and $1/\lambda$, one bigger than $1$. That direction grows without bound. The fixed point is hyperbolic (unstable): orbits fly off along a hyperbola.
  • $|a| = 2$ — the borderline, $\lambda = \pm 1$ repeated: marginal.

Drag $a$ across the two danger walls at $\pm 2$ and watch a seeded orbit switch from a bounded loop to an escaping hyperbola.

Key insight: For a symplectic map in the plane, linear stability is a single inequality, $|\operatorname{tr}\mathrm{d}T| < 2$. Cross the wall and a closed loop becomes an exponential escape — this is the discrete analogue of a stable centre turning into a saddle.

7.3 — How fast the angle turns: the rotation number

In the stable regime the eigenvalues are $e^{\pm i\mu}$, so each application of $T$ is (in suitable coordinates) a rotation by the angle $\mu$. Reading $\mu$ off the trace:

$$a = \operatorname{tr}\mathrm{d}T = \lambda + \lambda^{-1} = 2\cos\mu \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \mu = \arccos\!\frac{a}{2}.$$

Divide by a full turn to get the rotation number (accelerator physicists call it the tune) — the fraction of a circle advanced per iterate:

$$\nu_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi}\arccos\frac{a}{2}.$$

This is the discrete cousin of the winding frequency $\omega$ on the tori of Module 6, and the same rational/irrational drama returns. When $\nu_0 = m/n$ is rational, the orbit closes after exactly $n$ steps — a period-$n$ cycle. When $\nu_0$ is irrational, the orbit never closes and sprinkles points densely around a single loop. Drag $a$; the points are drawn one at a time so you can see them hop around by the angle $\mu$.

7.4 — The ellipse that is conserved: the Courant–Snyder invariant

Those closed loops are not just any curves — they are level sets of one quadratic function, and we can pin it down exactly. Guess a general quadratic $\mathcal K = \alpha q^2 + \beta qp + \gamma p^2$ and demand it be unchanged by the map, $\mathcal K[q',p'] = \mathcal K[q,p]$. Substituting $q'=p,\ p'=-q+ap$ and matching coefficients forces $\alpha=\gamma$ and $\beta = -a\alpha$, so up to scale

$$\boxed{\;\mathcal K_{\mathrm{CS}}[q,p] = p^2 - a\,p\,q + q^2\;}$$

is an exact invariant: $\mathcal K_{\mathrm{CS}}[T\zeta] = \mathcal K_{\mathrm{CS}}[\zeta]$ for every point. Let's check it directly — it takes one line:

$$p'^2 - a\,p'q' + q'^2 = (-q+ap)^2 - a(-q+ap)p + p^2 = q^2 - apq + p^2. \checkmark$$

This is the Courant–Snyder invariant of accelerator physics — the ellipse a beam particle rides turn after turn, whose area is the beam's emittance. Every orbit of §7.2–7.3 is one of its level curves. Drag $a$ to reshape the family of ellipses; click to drop an orbit and confirm it sits on a single level set.

One more observation will matter enormously later. If $\mathcal K_{\mathrm{CS}}$ is conserved, then so is any function of it — $\mathcal K_{\mathrm{CS}}^2$, $e^{\mathcal K_{\mathrm{CS}}}$, a whole power series $C_0\,\mathcal K_{\mathrm{CS}} + C_1\,\mathcal K_{\mathrm{CS}}^2 + \cdots$. The invariant of a map is never unique; it is only defined up to reparametrisation. That freedom is a nuisance now and a superpower later.

Where the story must go next. All of this rests on the map being linear. The real maps — a kicked rotor, a beam through nonlinear magnets, the Hénon map — carry higher powers, $p' = -q + a p + (\text{cubic, quintic}, \dots)$. Then no exact quadratic invariant survives, orbits stop being clean ellipses, and chaos leaks in near resonances. Two escape routes open up, and we take both: build the nonlinear map from a generator using the Lie-algebra machinery of Poisson brackets (Module 8), and construct an invariant that is conserved almost exactly (Module 10). The bridge from Module 4's brackets to modern research runs straight through here.
