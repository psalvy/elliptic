The Path Nature Chooses

Throw a ball across the room. Out of the infinitely many curves that connect where it left your hand to where it lands — straight lines, loops, wild zig-zags — the ball traces exactly one. Newton explains this locally: at every instant $F = ma$ nudges it. But there is a second, stranger explanation that looks at the whole trajectory at once and asks: of all conceivable paths between two fixed events, what makes the real one special?

The answer is a single number attached to each path — the action — and the rule that the true path makes it stationary. This module builds that rule from the ground up. Everything in the rest of the course is a consequence of it.

1.1 — A number for every path

Take a particle of mass $m$ moving in one dimension $x(t)$, in a uniform gravitational field so its potential energy is $V(x) = mgx$. At each instant it has a kinetic energy $T = \tfrac12 m\dot x^2$ and a potential energy $V$. Form their difference — this is the Lagrangian:

$$L(x, \dot x) = T - V = \tfrac12 m\dot x^2 - mgx.$$

The minus sign is not a typo and not obvious — we will see in §1.4 exactly why it must be a difference and not the sum. Now integrate $L$ along an entire candidate path from a fixed start event $(t_0, x_0)$ to a fixed end event $(t_1, x_1)$. That single number is the action:

$$S[x] = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} L\big(x(t), \dot x(t)\big)\, dt.$$

The square brackets in $S[x]$ are deliberate: $S$ is a functional, a machine that eats an entire function $x(t)$ and returns one real number. The trial path is the true path plus a bump that vanishes at both ends, $x_\alpha(t) = x_{\text{true}}(t) + \alpha\sin\!\big(\pi t / t_1\big)$. As you sweep $\alpha$, the action traces a parabola whose minimum sits exactly at $\alpha = 0$ — the true path. Push the path up or down, fast or slow, and $S$ only ever increases.

Key insight: The real trajectory is not the one that minimises energy, or distance, or time. It is the one that makes the action $S = \int (T - V)\,dt$ stationary against every small wiggle of the path.

1.2 — "Stationary," not "minimum"

The example above is genuinely a minimum — for a uniform force the action always is. But that is special. The honest statement of Hamilton's principle is only that the action is stationary: its first-order change vanishes for every small deformation, exactly as a function's derivative vanishes at a max, a min, or a saddle. A harmonic oscillator followed for longer than half a period, for instance, sits at a genuine saddle of $S$ — stationary, but not minimal. So we never say "least action," only "stationary action."

Make this precise. Perturb the true path $x(t)$ by an arbitrary small function $\eta(t)$ that respects the fixed endpoints, $\eta(t_0) = \eta(t_1) = 0$, scaled by a bookkeeping parameter $\varepsilon$:

$$x_\varepsilon(t) = x(t) + \varepsilon\,\eta(t).$$

Then $S(\varepsilon) = S[x_\varepsilon]$ is an ordinary function of the one real number $\varepsilon$. Stationarity is the single clean condition

$$\left.\frac{dS}{d\varepsilon}\right|_{\varepsilon = 0} = 0 \quad\text{for every admissible } \eta(t).$$

This object — the first-order response of a functional to a deformation — is the first variation, written $\delta S$. The whole content of mechanics is hidden in "$\delta S = 0$." Let us extract it.

1.3 — Deriving the Euler–Lagrange equation

Differentiate $S(\varepsilon) = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} L\big(x + \varepsilon\eta,\; \dot x + \varepsilon\dot\eta\big)\,dt$ under the integral sign. By the chain rule, the integrand's $\varepsilon$-derivative is

$$\frac{d}{d\varepsilon} L = \frac{\partial L}{\partial x}\,\eta + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x}\,\dot\eta.$$

Setting $\varepsilon = 0$ (so $x_\varepsilon \to x$, the true path) and demanding the integral vanish:

$$\delta S = \int_{t_0}^{t_1}\!\left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial x}\,\eta + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x}\,\dot\eta \right) dt = 0.$$

The two terms treat $\eta$ differently — one multiplies $\eta$, the other its derivative $\dot\eta$. We cannot yet factor out $\eta$. The fix is integration by parts on the second term, which trades the derivative off $\eta$:

$$\int_{t_0}^{t_1} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x}\,\dot\eta\, dt = \underbrace{\left[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x}\,\eta \right]_{t_0}^{t_1}}_{\text{boundary term}} - \int_{t_0}^{t_1} \frac{d}{dt}\!\left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x} \right)\eta\, dt.$$

Here is where the fixed endpoints earn their keep: since $\eta(t_0) = \eta(t_1) = 0$, the boundary term is exactly zero. Substituting back, the deformation $\eta$ now appears as a common factor:

$$\delta S = \int_{t_0}^{t_1}\!\left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial x} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x} \right)\eta(t)\, dt = 0.$$

This must hold for every admissible $\eta$. A continuous function whose weighted integral against all test functions vanishes must itself be identically zero — the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations. (If the bracket were positive on some interval, choose an $\eta$ that bumps up only there; the integral would be positive, contradiction.) Therefore the bracket vanishes pointwise:

$$\boxed{\;\frac{d}{dt}\!\left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x} \right) = \frac{\partial L}{\partial x}\;}$$

This is the Euler–Lagrange equation. One equation per coordinate; for $n$ coordinates $q_1, \dots, q_n$ it reads $\frac{d}{dt}(\partial L/\partial \dot q_i) = \partial L/\partial q_i$ with no change to the derivation.

Key insight: Integration by parts is the entire trick. It moves the time-derivative off the arbitrary wiggle $\eta$ and onto the physics, and the fixed endpoints kill the leftover boundary term. What remains must vanish for all $\eta$ — so it vanishes identically.

1.4 — Sanity check: where is $F = ma$?

A new principle is worthless if it does not reproduce what we already trust. Feed our Lagrangian $L = \tfrac12 m\dot x^2 - V(x)$ into the Euler–Lagrange machine. The two pieces are

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x} = m\dot x, \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = -\frac{dV}{dx}.$$

The first is just the momentum $p = m\dot x$ — remember that it fell out of $\partial L/\partial \dot x$; that is no coincidence, and Module 2 is built on it. Plugging both into $\frac{d}{dt}(\partial L/\partial\dot x) = \partial L/\partial x$:

$$\frac{d}{dt}(m\dot x) = -\frac{dV}{dx} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; m\ddot x = -V'(x) = F.$$

Exactly Newton's second law. Now the minus sign in $L = T - V$ is forced: only a difference produces $+\partial T/\partial\dot x$ on the left (the momentum's rate of change) and $-\partial V/\partial x$ on the right (the force). A sum would put a spurious $+V'$ and give the wrong sign of force. Hamilton's principle is not a re-skin of Newton — it is a deeper statement that implies Newton, while also extending cleanly to fields, relativity, and quantum mechanics, where forces are awkward but actions are natural.

Why this reformulation matters: $F = ma$ is a vector equation tied to Cartesian coordinates. The Euler–Lagrange equation has the same form in any coordinates — polar, rotating, constrained-to-a-surface — because the action is just a number and does not care how you label the path. Choosing good coordinates becomes the whole game.

1.5 — The thread to pull next

The Euler–Lagrange equation is second order in time: to launch a trajectory you must specify both a position $x$ and a velocity $\dot x$. Yet something kept trying to simplify itself. The combination $\partial L/\partial \dot x$ appeared twice — once as the thing we differentiate, once as the momentum $p = m\dot x$. What if we stopped treating velocity as fundamental and promoted this $p$ to a coordinate in its own right?

Doing so trades one second-order equation for two first-order equations of breathtaking symmetry — and the transformation that accomplishes it, the Legendre transform, is a beautiful piece of geometry in its own right. That is Module 2.
