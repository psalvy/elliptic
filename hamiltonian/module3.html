Life in Phase Space

Module 2 reduced all of mechanics to a point $(q, p)$ and an instruction for where it goes next:

$$\dot q = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}, \qquad \dot p = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}.$$

Read those two numbers $(\dot q, \dot p)$ as an arrow attached to each point of the plane, and mechanics becomes a single picture: a vector field, and the curves that thread through it. This plane — one axis for position, one for momentum — is phase space, and learning to read it is learning to see a dynamical system whole.

3.1 — The state is a point; the law is a flow

Take the pendulum, the workhorse of the rest of the course. With mass, length and gravity scaled to one, its Hamiltonian is

$$H(q, p) = \tfrac12 p^2 + \big(1 - \cos q\big),$$

where $q$ is the angle from straight down and $p$ the angular momentum. Hamilton's equations give the arrow at every point:

$$\dot q = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p} = p, \qquad \dot p = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q} = -\sin q.$$

3.2 — Why orbits are level sets of $H$

The orbits are not arbitrary curves. Watch how the energy of the system changes as it moves, by differentiating $H$ along a trajectory and substituting Hamilton's equations:

$$\frac{dH}{dt} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial q}\dot q + \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}\dot p = \frac{\partial H}{\partial q}\frac{\partial H}{\partial p} + \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}\!\left(-\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}\right) = 0.$$

The two terms are identical except for a sign — that lone minus from Hamilton's equations — so they cancel exactly. $H$ is conserved. Energy conservation is not an extra assumption bolted on; it is a one-line consequence of the antisymmetry built into the equations. (This holds for any $H$ with no explicit time dependence.)

Geometrically, conservation of $H$ means every trajectory is confined to a curve $H(q,p) = E$ — a level set of the energy. That is why the orbits you launch always close up smoothly and trace the contours drawn faintly in the background: the flow can shuttle a state around a level set but never off it.

Key insight: The vector field of Hamilton's equations is everywhere tangent to the level sets of $H$. Time evolution slides the state along a contour of constant energy — never across one.

3.3 — Three kinds of orbit, one picture

For the pendulum the level sets split into three regimes, all visible at once in the phase portrait:

  • Libration ($E < 2$): closed loops around the origin. The pendulum swings back and forth. Small loops are near-ellipses — for tiny swings $1 - \cos q \approx \tfrac12 q^2$ and the pendulum is a harmonic oscillator.
  • The separatrix ($E = 2$): the special orbit that just barely reaches the top. It takes infinite time to arrive, so it is an open curve that never quite closes — the boundary between two behaviours.
  • Rotation ($E > 2$): the bob has enough energy to go over the top and round and round. These orbits run off the right and reappear on the left, because $q$ and $q + 2\pi$ are the same angle.

The points where the field vanishes are the fixed points. At $(0,0)$ the pendulum hangs at rest — a centre, with orbits circling it stably. At $(\pm\pi, 0)$ it balances inverted — a saddle, where two branches of the separatrix cross; the slightest push sends it away. Launch an orbit right next to the saddle to feel how it lingers, then bolts.

3.4 — Why trajectories never cross

Notice you can never make two orbits intersect at an angle. This is forced, not lucky. At any point the field defines one arrow, so there is exactly one direction the flow can take. If two distinct trajectories crossed, the state at the crossing would have two futures — impossible for a deterministic first-order system. This is the geometric face of the existence-and-uniqueness theorem for ODEs, and it is why the phase portrait neatly tiles into nested, non-crossing curves.

Reading a phase portrait: closed loops ⇒ oscillation; nested loops shrinking to a point ⇒ a stable equilibrium at the centre; crossing separatrices ⇒ an unstable equilibrium; curves that march off to infinity ⇒ unbounded motion. The shape of $H$'s contours is the qualitative dynamics.

3.5 — The thread to pull next

We found one conserved quantity, $H$ itself, and it pinned every orbit to a curve. That single constant of motion was the difference between a comprehensible picture and a tangle. It raises a sharp question: are there other conserved quantities hiding in a given system, and is there a mechanical way to find them rather than guessing?

There is, and it comes from a remarkable bracket operation $\{f, H\}$ that computes the rate of change of any quantity along the flow — and turns "is this conserved?" into a one-line calculation. But first, a playground to make everything so far second nature.
