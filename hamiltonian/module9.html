The Hénon Map & Reversibility

We now meet the map the target paper is built around, and uncover the symmetry that survives even when every conserved quantity has been destroyed. Module 7 gave us symplectic maps; Module 8 gave us the algebra that generates them. Here is the concrete, chaotic, beautiful object they were preparing us for — and the discrete echo of Noether's theorem hiding inside it.

9.1 — The cubic Hénon map: order and chaos on one screen

Take the linear map of Module 7 and make the kick nonlinear. In the McMillan form — position and momentum swap roles, with a force $f$ acting on the momentum —

$$T:\qquad q' = p, \qquad p' = -q + f(p), \qquad f(p) = p + p^3.$$

the extra cubic term $p^3$ changes everything. The origin is still a stable fixed point (the linearised map has trace $f'(0)=1<2$, tune $\nu_0 = \tfrac16$), but two unstable fixed points now sit at $(1,1)$ and $(-1,-1)$ — solve $q=p$ and $p=-q+p+p^3$ to get $p^3=p$. Their stable and unstable manifolds cross in a tangle, and an exact invariant $\mathcal K[T\zeta]=\mathcal K[\zeta]$ no longer exists. 9.2 — Reversibility: the map is two mirrors

Even with no conserved quantity, the map is not structureless. It is reversible: it factors into two reflections, each of which undoes itself. Define two involutions (maps with $R^2 = \mathrm{Id}$):

$$R_1:\ (q,p)\mapsto(p,q), \qquad R_2:\ (q,p)\mapsto\big(q,\,-p+f(q)\big).$$

$R_1$ is reflection across the diagonal $p=q$; $R_2$ is a vertical flip about the curve $p=f(q)/2$. A one-line check shows the Hénon map is exactly their composition:

$$R_2\big(R_1(q,p)\big) = R_2(p,q) = \big(p,\,-q+f(p)\big) = T(q,p). \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad T = R_2\circ R_1.$$

Because $R_1^2 = R_2^2 = \mathrm{Id}$, running the map backward is just $T^{-1} = R_1\circ R_2$ — same two mirrors, opposite order. The fixed-point sets of the reflections are the symmetry lines:

$$\ell_1 = \mathrm{Fix}(R_1):\ p = q, \qquad \ell_2 = \mathrm{Fix}(R_2):\ p = \tfrac12 f(q).$$

Two consequences you can see below. First, the entire phase portrait is symmetric under $R_1$ — reflect any orbit across the diagonal and you land on another orbit (the same one, run backward). Second, all the symmetric fixed points and cycles are forced to sit on the symmetry lines — which is why $(0,0)$ and $(\pm1,\pm1)$ all lie on $\ell_1$. 9.3 — Reversibility is the discrete Noether

Step back to Module 4. Noether's theorem said: every continuous symmetry of the action yields an exact conserved quantity — time-translation gives energy, rotation gives angular momentum. That is the engine of integrability.

The reflections $R_1, R_2$ are a discrete symmetry. They generate an infinite group (all the words $R_1 R_2 R_1 \cdots$), and unlike the continuous case they persist in chaos, where exact invariants have been destroyed. The organising principle of the target paper is the parallel between these two worlds:

Noether (Module 4)Reversibility (this paper)
Continuous symmetry of the action Discrete symmetry: reversing involutions $R_1, R_2$
Yields an exact invariant $\dot I = 0$ Yields an approximate invariant $\mathcal K[T\zeta]-\mathcal K[\zeta]=\mathcal O(\epsilon^{n+1})$
Guarantees a smooth conserved surface The approximate invariant inherits the discrete symmetry — its level sets respect $R_1, R_2$
Works only for integrable systems Works even in near-integrable and chaotic maps

That is the thesis: reversibility plays for discrete maps the structural role that continuous symmetry plays for flows. It cannot hand us a perfect conserved quantity — chaos forbids that — but it constrains and shapes an almost-conserved one, order by order. Building that object is the last step, and the doorway to the research paper itself.

How far does the analogy actually go?

Far enough to organise the whole subject — but it is a deliberate parallel, not a single theorem in disguise, and it is worth being exact about the seam. Noether's mechanism is tight and needs continuity: a continuous symmetry has an infinitesimal generator $G$ with $\{G,H\}=0$, and that same equation reads $\dot G = 0$ — so the generator of the symmetry is the conserved quantity. A reflection has no infinitesimal generator, so this move is unavailable. Reversibility ($R\,T\,R = T^{-1}$) yields no exact conserved quantity at all — indeed it is just time-reversal, which was never a Noether symmetry even for the original flow.

So two different structures split the work that Noether does with one:

  • Near-integrability — a non-resonant elliptic fixed point — is what actually makes an approximate invariant exist (the Birkhoff normal-form series of Module 8). This would run even without reversibility.
  • Reversibility does not produce the invariant; it shapes it — pinning symmetric orbits to $\ell_1,\ell_2$, forcing every invariant to respect the reflections, and halving the unknowns so the construction is tractable.

The genuine common root is only the broad principle "symmetry constrains the invariant structures of a system." Continuous-plus-Hamiltonian is strong enough to manufacture an exact invariant for free; a discrete symmetry is weaker and merely organises an approximate one built by other means. The paper's move is to engineer $\mathcal K^{(n)}$ so that it inherits the discrete symmetry and thereby occupies Noether's structural slot — a real and useful rhyme, not a hidden identity.
