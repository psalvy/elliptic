Galois Acting on Torsion Points

5.1 — Torsion Points of an Elliptic Curve

Let $E$ be an elliptic curve defined over $\mathbb{Q}$, say $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$ with $a, b \in \mathbb{Q}$. For any integer $n \geq 1$, the $n$-torsion subgroup is:

$$E[n] = \{P \in E(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}) : nP = \mathcal{O}\}$$

the set of points (over the algebraic closure $\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$) killed by $n$ under the group law. These are the "division points" of the curve.

From the torus picture of Module 3 of the elliptic course: $E(\mathbb{C}) \cong \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ where $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}\omega_1 \oplus \mathbb{Z}\omega_2$. Under this isomorphism, the $n$-torsion points are exactly:

$$E[n] \leftrightarrow \frac{1}{n}\Lambda/\Lambda = \left\{\frac{a\omega_1 + b\omega_2}{n} : a, b \in \{0, 1, \ldots, n-1\}\right\}$$

This is a $2D$ grid of $n^2$ points. As an abstract group:

$$E[n] \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$$

5.2 — Torsion Coordinates Are Algebraic

The $x$-coordinates of $n$-torsion points satisfy the $n$-th division polynomial $\psi_n(x)$, a polynomial with coefficients in $\mathbb{Q}$ (or the field of definition of $E$). So torsion points have algebraic coordinates — they live in some finite extension of $\mathbb{Q}$.

This means $\text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$ acts on the coordinates of torsion points: if $P = (x_P, y_P) \in E[n]$ and $\sigma \in \text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$, then $\sigma(P) = (\sigma(x_P), \sigma(y_P))$ is again a point in $E[n]$. Crucially, Galois respects the group law: $\sigma(P + Q) = \sigma(P) + \sigma(Q)$ (because the addition formulas have rational coefficients).

So each $\sigma$ gives a group automorphism of $E[n] \cong (\mathbb{Z}/n)^2$. Group automorphisms of $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^2$ are exactly invertible $2 \times 2$ matrices over $\mathbb{Z}/n$.

5.3 — The Mod-$n$ Galois Representation

Choose a basis $\{P_1, P_2\}$ of $E[n]$ (two independent $n$-torsion points). Each $\sigma \in \text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$ gives a matrix:

$$\sigma(P_1) = a P_1 + b P_2, \quad \sigma(P_2) = c P_1 + d P_2 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \rho_n(\sigma) = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in \text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})$$

This defines the mod-$n$ Galois representation:

$$\rho_n : \text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \to \text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})$$

It is a group homomorphism (because Galois acts on coordinates, so composition of automorphisms corresponds to matrix multiplication). Different bases give conjugate representations; the image is well-defined up to conjugacy.

5.4 — The Weil Pairing Constraint

There is a canonical bilinear, alternating, non-degenerate pairing:

$$e_n : E[n] \times E[n] \to \mu_n \qquad (\text{$n$-th roots of unity})$$

called the Weil pairing. It is preserved by Galois: $e_n(\sigma P, \sigma Q) = \sigma(e_n(P,Q))$. Since $\sigma$ acts on $\mu_n$ by the cyclotomic character $\chi_n(\sigma)$ (where $\sigma(\zeta) = \zeta^{\chi_n(\sigma)}$ for $\zeta$ a primitive $n$-th root of unity), this forces:

$$\det(\rho_n(\sigma)) = \chi_n(\sigma)$$

So the image of $\rho_n$ lies not in all of $\text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/n)$ but in the symplectic subgroup $\text{GSp}_2(\mathbb{Z}/n)$ (matrices with determinant equal to the cyclotomic character).

5.5 — Serre's Theorem and the Size of the Image

For a "generic" elliptic curve $E$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ (one without special symmetries — specifically without Complex Multiplication), Serre proved in 1972 that:

Serre's Open Image Theorem (1972): If $E/\mathbb{Q}$ has no CM, then for all but finitely many primes $\ell$, the representation $\rho_\ell : \text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \to \text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/\ell)$ is surjective. Galois acts as freely as possible on the torsion.

The image is as large as it can be (subject to the Weil pairing constraint). This is the "generic" case — no extra structure.

CM curves are the exception: they have extra endomorphisms that commute with Galois, forcing the image to be much smaller — in fact, abelian. This is the bridge to Module 6.

5.6 — The $\ell$-adic Representation

For a prime $\ell$, consider all the torsion: $E[\ell^k]$ for all $k \geq 1$. Passing to the inverse limit gives the $\ell$-adic Tate module:

$$T_\ell E = \varprojlim_k E[\ell^k] \cong \mathbb{Z}_\ell^2$$

and the $\ell$-adic Galois representation:

$$\rho_\ell : \text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \to \text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$$

This is the object that appears in Wiles's proof of Fermat's Last Theorem (1995): the representation attached to a hypothetical Fermat solution would contradict modularity, establishing a contradiction.
