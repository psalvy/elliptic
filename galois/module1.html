Field Extensions: Adding a Root

1.1 — A Problem with Rationals

Consider the equation $x^2 - 2 = 0$. Its solutions are $\pm\sqrt{2}$. Neither is rational — there is no fraction $p/q$ whose square is $2$. The rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ are incomplete for this polynomial.

The fix: adjoin a root. We formally add $\sqrt{2}$ to $\mathbb{Q}$ and close under the field operations. Every product $\sqrt{2}\cdot\sqrt{2} = 2$ lands back in $\mathbb{Q}$, so the result stays finite:

$$\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) = \{a + b\sqrt{2} : a, b \in \mathbb{Q}\}$$

This is a field: addition and multiplication close within it, and every non-zero element has an inverse (rationalise: $1/(a+b\sqrt{2}) = (a-b\sqrt{2})/(a^2-2b^2)$ when $a^2 \neq 2b^2$).

1.2 — Field Extensions and Degree

$\mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ is a field extension. As a vector space over $\mathbb{Q}$, it has basis $\{1, \sqrt{2}\}$ — dimension 2. The degree of the extension:

$$[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 2$$

In general: for fields $F \subset K$, the degree $[K : F]$ is the dimension of $K$ as an $F$-vector space. It measures "how much bigger" $K$ is than $F$. Key examples:

Extension Basis over $\mathbb{Q}$ Degree
$\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$$\{1,\, \sqrt{2}\}$2
$\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$$\{1,\, \sqrt[3]{2},\, \sqrt[3]{4}\}$3
$\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3})$$\{1,\, \sqrt{2},\, \sqrt{3},\, \sqrt{6}\}$4
$\mathbb{Q}(i)$$\{1,\, i\}$2

1.3 — The Minimal Polynomial

An element $\alpha$ is algebraic over $F$ if it satisfies some polynomial with $F$-coefficients. Its minimal polynomial $\text{min}_F(\alpha)$ is the unique monic, irreducible polynomial over $F$ that $\alpha$ satisfies.

Key fact: $[F(\alpha) : F] = \deg(\text{min}_F(\alpha))$.

  • $\sqrt{2}$ has minimal polynomial $x^2 - 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ → degree 2
  • $\sqrt[3]{2}$ has minimal polynomial $x^3 - 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ (irreducible by Eisenstein at $p=2$) → degree 3
  • $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$ has minimal polynomial $x^2 + x + 1$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ → degree 2
  • $\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}$ has minimal polynomial $x^4 - 10x^2 + 1$ → degree 4
Deep Dive: Eisenstein's Criterion

A polynomial $f(x) = a_n x^n + \cdots + a_0 \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ if there exists a prime $p$ such that:

  • $p \nmid a_n$ (leading coefficient)
  • $p \mid a_i$ for all $i < n$ (all other coefficients)
  • $p^2 \nmid a_0$ (constant term not doubly divisible)

For $x^3 - 2$ with $p = 2$: leading coefficient 1 not divisible by 2 ✓; coefficients of $x^2, x$ are 0 ✓; constant $-2$ divisible by 2 but not 4 ✓. So $x^3 - 2$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$, confirming $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}):\mathbb{Q}] = 3$.

1.4 — The Tower Law

If $F \subseteq M \subseteq K$ are fields with finite extensions, degrees multiply:

$$[K : F] = [K : M] \cdot [M : F]$$

This is the Tower Law. It follows from the fact that if $\{v_i\}$ is a basis of $M/F$ and $\{w_j\}$ is a basis of $K/M$, then $\{v_i w_j\}$ is a basis of $K/F$.

Example: $\mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}) \subset \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)$. The first step has degree 3; the second has degree 2 (adjoin $\omega$, whose minimal polynomial $x^2+x+1$ stays irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$ since $\omega \notin \mathbb{R}$). Tower Law: $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega) : \mathbb{Q}] = 3 \cdot 2 = 6$. This is the splitting field of $x^3 - 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$.

1.5 — Splitting Fields

The splitting field of a polynomial $f$ over $F$ is the smallest extension of $F$ containing all roots of $f$. It is the natural home for Galois theory: every symmetry of the roots is an automorphism of the splitting field.

The splitting field of $x^3 - 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ is $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)$ of degree 6. Its Galois group — the group of automorphisms fixing $\mathbb{Q}$ — will be $S_3$, the symmetric group on 3 elements. This is Module 2's story.
