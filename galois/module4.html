Solvability: Why the Quintic Breaks

4.1 — Solving by Radicals

The quadratic formula $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$ expresses the roots using only arithmetic and square roots. For cubics (Cardano, 1545) and quartics (Ferrari, 1540s), similar formulas exist using nested radicals. The natural question: is there a formula for degree 5?

Abel (1824) proved: no. Galois (1832) explained exactly why — and the answer is group-theoretic.

A polynomial $f(x)$ is solvable by radicals if its roots can be expressed by a finite chain of additions, subtractions, multiplications, divisions, and $n$-th root extractions starting from the coefficients. In terms of field extensions: the splitting field $K$ is contained in a tower $F = F_0 \subset F_1 \subset \cdots \subset F_m$ where each step $F_{i+1} = F_i(\alpha_i)$ with $\alpha_i^{n_i} \in F_i$ (a radical extension).

4.2 — Solvable Groups

A group $G$ is solvable if there is a subnormal series:

$$G = G_0 \supset G_1 \supset G_2 \supset \cdots \supset G_k = \{e\}$$

where each quotient $G_i/G_{i+1}$ is abelian. Equivalently: $G$ can be "dismantled" into commutative layers. The derived series is the canonical choice: $G^{(0)} = G$, $G^{(i+1)} = [G^{(i)}, G^{(i)}]$ (the commutator subgroup). $G$ is solvable iff $G^{(k)} = \{e\}$ for some $k$.

Galois's criterion: $f(x)$ is solvable by radicals over $\mathbb{Q}$ $\Leftrightarrow$ $\text{Gal}(f) = \text{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q})$ is a solvable group.

4.3 — Lower Degrees Are Solvable

  • Degree 2: $\text{Gal}(f) \leq S_2 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2$ (abelian) → solvable → quadratic formula
  • Degree 3: $\text{Gal}(f) \leq S_3$. The derived series: $[S_3, S_3] = A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/3$, $[A_3, A_3] = \{e\}$. The quotients are $S_3/A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2$ and $A_3/\{e\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/3$ — both abelian → solvable → Cardano's cubic formula
  • Degree 4: $\text{Gal}(f) \leq S_4$. Derived series: $[S_4,S_4] = A_4$, $[A_4,A_4] = V_4 = \{e,(12)(34),(13)(24),(14)(23)\}$, $[V_4,V_4] = \{e\}$. All quotients abelian → solvable → Ferrari's quartic formula

4.4 — The Quintic is Not Solvable: $A_5$ is Simple

For degree 5, a "generic" polynomial (one with no special symmetries) has $\text{Gal}(f) = S_5$. The derived series of $S_5$:

$$S_5 \supset A_5 \supset A_5 \supset A_5 \supset \cdots$$

The derived series gets stuck at $A_5$ because $[A_5, A_5] = A_5$ — the alternating group $A_5$ is perfect (equals its own commutator subgroup). In fact, $A_5$ is simple: it has no proper normal subgroups at all.

Proof that $A_5$ is simple (sketch): $A_5$ has order 60. A normal subgroup would have order dividing 60. The 5-cycles, 3-cycles, and double transpositions each form conjugacy classes of sizes 24, 20, 15, and 1. A normal subgroup is a union of conjugacy classes including $\{e\}$. Checking all subsets: no union of those class sizes (1, 15, 20, 24) adds to a proper divisor of 60. Therefore no proper normal subgroup exists.

Since $A_5$ is non-abelian and simple, the derived series of $S_5$ never reaches $\{e\}$. So $S_5$ is not solvable. Therefore a generic degree-5 polynomial with Galois group $S_5$ is not solvable by radicals.

Abel–Ruffini theorem: There is no general formula (using radicals) for the roots of a degree-5 polynomial. This is a theorem about groups, not polynomials — the obstruction is the simplicity of $A_5$.
Deep Dive: Specific Quintics That Are Solvable

Not every quintic is unsolvable — only those with Galois group $S_5$ or $A_5$. A quintic with Galois group $\mathbb{Z}/5$ (a cyclic group) IS solvable. For example, $x^5 - 2$ has Galois group a subgroup of $\text{AGL}(1, \mathbb{F}_5) \cong \mathbb{Z}/5 \rtimes \mathbb{Z}/4$ — solvable. The roots $\sqrt[5]{2} \cdot \zeta_5^k$ can be expressed using 5th and 4th roots: that's a solvable tower.

The "generic" quintic $x^5 + ax + b$ for most choices of $a, b$ has Galois group $S_5$, which is the unsolvable case. It is a theorem that solvable quintics are the exception, not the rule.

The deeper picture: The solvability of a polynomial depends only on its Galois group — the abstract symmetry group of its roots. The formula vs. no-formula dichotomy is really a statement about whether this symmetry group can be "peeled apart" into abelian layers. Galois theory reduces a question about polynomial arithmetic to a question about group structure.
