CM, Class Fields, and the Abelian Thread

6.1 — The Generic Picture: Serre's Theorem

Recall from Module 5 the mod-$n$ Galois representation:

$$\rho_n : \text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \to \text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})$$

The Weil pairing forces the image into $\text{GSp}_2$ (determinant = cyclotomic character), but beyond that constraint, how big is the image?

Serre's Open Image Theorem (1972): For a "generic" elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ — meaning $\text{End}(E) = \mathbb{Z}$, no complex multiplication — the image of $\rho_n$ is surjective onto $\text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})$ for all but finitely many $n$. Passing to the $\ell$-adic limit, the image of $\rho_\ell : \text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \to \text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ is an open subgroup of $\text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$.

In other words: Galois acts on the torsion as freely as possible. The torsion points of a generic curve are "as algebraically independent as they can be given the Weil constraint." To adjoin all $n$-torsion coordinates of a generic curve to $\mathbb{Q}$ gives a highly non-abelian extension of $\mathbb{Q}$.

Non-abelian: $\text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/\ell\mathbb{Z})$ is non-abelian for $\ell \geq 3$. Serre's theorem says the field $\mathbb{Q}(E[\ell])$ generated by torsion coordinates has Galois group $\cong \text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/\ell\mathbb{Z})$ generically. This is very far from the abelian extensions that class field theory describes.

6.2 — Complex Multiplication: Extra Endomorphisms

An elliptic curve $E$ over $\mathbb{C}$ is a torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ where $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}\omega_1 + \mathbb{Z}\omega_2$. The endomorphism ring $\text{End}(E)$ consists of complex numbers $\alpha$ with $\alpha\Lambda \subseteq \Lambda$.

For a generic lattice, $\alpha\Lambda \subseteq \Lambda$ forces $\alpha \in \mathbb{Z}$: only multiplication-by-$n$ maps. But for special lattices, extra $\alpha$ exist.

Example: Take $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}i$ (the Gaussian integers). Then $\alpha = i$ satisfies $i\Lambda = i\mathbb{Z} + i^2\mathbb{Z} = i\mathbb{Z} - \mathbb{Z} \subseteq \Lambda$. So $\text{End}(E) \supseteq \mathbb{Z}[i]$ — the Gaussian integers, an order in $\mathbb{Q}(i)$.

In general: $E$ has complex multiplication (CM) if $\text{End}(E) \supsetneq \mathbb{Z}$. The extra endomorphisms form an order $\mathcal{O}$ in an imaginary quadratic field $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$:

$$\text{End}(E) \cong \mathcal{O} \subset \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$$

The CM value of $\tau = \omega_2/\omega_1$ lies in the upper half-plane with $\tau \in \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$ — a quadratic irrationality. The corresponding $j$-invariant $j(\tau)$ is then an algebraic integer of a very special type.

CM examples:
  • $\tau = i$: $\text{End}(E) \cong \mathbb{Z}[i]$, $j(i) = 1728$
  • $\tau = e^{2\pi i/3} = \frac{-1+\sqrt{-3}}{2}$: $\text{End}(E) \cong \mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ (Eisenstein integers), $j(\omega) = 0$
  • $\tau = \frac{1+\sqrt{-7}}{2}$: $j(\tau) = -3375 = -15^3$
  • $\tau = \sqrt{-2}$: $j(\tau) = 8000 = 20^3$
  • $\tau = \frac{1+\sqrt{-163}}{2}$: $j(\tau) = -262537412640768000 \approx -e^{\pi\sqrt{163}}$

All integers — and for discriminants with class number 1, they are rational integers. This is not a coincidence.

6.3 — Why CM Forces Abelian Galois Action

Here is the key argument. Fix an $\ell$-torsion point $P \in E[\ell]$ and a CM endomorphism $[\alpha] \in \text{End}(E)$ with $\alpha \in \mathcal{O} \subset K$. For any $\sigma \in \text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$:

$$\sigma([\alpha](P)) = [\alpha](\sigma(P))$$

This is because $[\alpha]$ is defined over $K$ (its coefficients are in $K$, the CM field), and if we restrict to $\sigma \in \text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/K)$ (fixing $K$), then $\sigma$ commutes with $[\alpha]$. In matrix terms:

$$\rho_\ell(\sigma) \cdot \rho_\ell([\alpha]) = \rho_\ell([\alpha]) \cdot \rho_\ell(\sigma) \quad \forall \sigma \in \text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/K)$$

The matrix $\rho_\ell([\alpha])$ (the action of the CM endomorphism on $E[\ell] \cong (\mathbb{Z}/\ell)^2$) commutes with the entire image of $\text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/K)$. By Schur's lemma-type reasoning: this forces the image of $\text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/K) \to \text{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/\ell)$ to land in the centraliser of $\mathcal{O}/\ell\mathcal{O}$ inside $\text{GL}_2$.

That centraliser is isomorphic to $(\mathcal{O}/\ell\mathcal{O})^\times$ — a commutative (abelian) group. The image of Galois is therefore abelian.

The punchline: For a CM curve $E$ with CM by $\mathcal{O} \subset K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$:
$$\rho_\ell(\text{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/K)) \subseteq (\mathcal{O}/\ell\mathcal{O})^\times \cong \text{(abelian group)}$$
The Galois action on torsion — which is non-abelian for generic curves — collapses to an abelian one. This is the "abelian thread" of the title.

6.4 — Class Field Theory in One Paragraph

The Kronecker–Weber theorem says: every abelian extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ is contained in a cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$ for some $n$. The torsion points of the multiplicative group $\mathbb{G}_m$ — the roots of unity $\mu_n$ — generate all abelian extensions of $\mathbb{Q}$.

Class field theory generalises this. For a number field $K$, abelian extensions of $K$ are classified by the ideal class group (and its generalisations, the ray class groups). The reciprocity map is the Artin map:

$$\text{Cl}(K) \xrightarrow{\sim} \text{Gal}(H/K)$$

where $H$ is the Hilbert class field of $K$ — the maximal abelian unramified extension. The class number $h = |\text{Cl}(K)|$ equals $[H : K]$.

For imaginary quadratic fields $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$: the class group encodes the obstruction to unique factorisation in $\mathcal{O}_K$. When $h = 1$, $K$ has unique factorisation and $H = K$ — no extension is needed.

6.5 — Kronecker's Jugendtraum

Kronecker's "dream of youth" (Jugendtraum) was to find explicit generators for all abelian extensions of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$, analogous to how $\zeta_n = e^{2\pi i/n}$ generates abelian extensions of $\mathbb{Q}$.

Theorem (CM theory, realised by Weber, Takagi, Shimura): Let $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$ with ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$. Then:

  1. $j(\tau)$ is algebraic over $K$ (in fact over $\mathbb{Q}$) for any $\tau \in K \cap \mathfrak{H}$.
  2. $K(j(\tau))$ is the Hilbert class field $H$ of $K$.
  3. The torsion coordinates of any CM elliptic curve with CM by $\mathcal{O}_K$ generate the maximal abelian extension $K^{ab}$ of $K$.

Explicitly: the role played by $e^{2\pi i/n}$ for $\mathbb{Q}$ is played by $j(\tau)$ and torsion point coordinates for $K$. The torus $\mathbb{C}/\mathcal{O}_K$ is the analogue of the multiplicative group $\mathbb{C}^\times$, and its torsion points (the $n$-division points) generate abelian extensions.

6.6 — Why CM Forces $j(\tau) \in \mathbb{Z}$ When $h = 1$

This closes the loop with Module 7 of the elliptic course, where we used the fact that $j(i) = 1728$, $j(\omega) = 0$, etc., without fully explaining why these are integers.

The argument:

  1. By CM theory, $j(\tau) \in H = K(j(\tau))$ and $K(j(\tau))$ is the Hilbert class field of $K$.
  2. When $h = |\text{Cl}(K)| = 1$, the class group is trivial, so the Hilbert class field is $H = K$ itself: $[H : K] = h = 1$.
  3. Therefore $j(\tau) \in K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$.
  4. But $j(\tau)$ is also an algebraic integer (it satisfies a monic polynomial with integer coefficients — its minimal polynomial over $\mathbb{Q}$ has degree $h = 1$).
  5. An algebraic integer in $\mathbb{Q}$ is an ordinary integer. So $j(\tau) \in \mathbb{Z}$.
The nine Heegner numbers: The imaginary quadratic fields $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$ with class number 1 are exactly $d = 1, 2, 3, 7, 11, 19, 43, 67, 163$. For each, the corresponding $j(\tau)$ is an integer — the "Heegner points" of the $j$-line. Their values range from $j(i) = 1728$ to $j\!\left(\frac{1+\sqrt{-163}}{2}\right) = -262537412640768000$.

When $h > 1$, $j(\tau)$ is still algebraic but no longer rational — it generates a degree-$h$ extension of $\mathbb{Q}$. The class group "splits" the $j$-values into a Galois conjugate set of size $h$, and their elementary symmetric functions (integers) give the minimal polynomial.

6.7 — Ramanujan's $\pi$ Formula: the Full Circle

We can now explain why Ramanujan's formula

$$\frac{1}{\pi} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{9801} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(4n)!}{(n!)^4} \cdot \frac{26390n + 1103}{396^{4n}}$$

works, and why the constants $9801 = 99^2$, $396 = 4 \cdot 99$, and $1103$ are not magic — they arise from the CM theory of $\tau = \frac{1+\sqrt{-163}}{2}$.

The key connection is the Chowla–Selberg formula: the periods of a CM elliptic curve can be expressed in terms of values of the gamma function at rational arguments. Specifically, for $E$ with CM by $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-163})$:

$$\frac{1}{\pi} \sim \frac{\text{(algebraic number involving } j(\tau)\text{)}}{\text{(product of } \Gamma \text{ values)}}$$

The Borwein brothers and Borwein–Borwein–Chudnovsky generalised this systematically. For each of the 9 Heegner discriminants, there is one such $\pi$ formula. The discriminant $-163$ gives the fastest-converging Ramanujan formula because $|j(\tau)|$ is largest there — each term gains $\sim \pi\sqrt{163} \approx 40$ digits.

The constants in the formula encode:

  • $396 = 4 \cdot 99$: related to $e^{\pi\sqrt{163}/3}$, which is nearly an integer
  • $1103$ and $26390$: arise from the expansion of $1/\sqrt{j(\tau)}$ around the CM point
  • $9801 = 99^2$: the square appears because the formula involves the real period squared

6.8 — The Abelian Thread: A Summary

The "abelian thread" running through this course and the elliptic course is:

Galois acts on torsion
↓ Generic: image is all of $\text{GL}_2$ (non-abelian, Serre)
↓ CM: image forced into centraliser of $\mathcal{O}$ (abelian!)
↓ Abelian Galois action ↔ class field theory (Kronecker–Weber analogue)
↓ $j(\tau)$ generates Hilbert class field $H/K$
↓ When $h = 1$: $H = K$, so $j(\tau) \in \mathbb{Q}$, and $j(\tau) \in \mathbb{Z}$ (algebraic integer)
↓ The CM periods satisfy algebraic relations (Chowla–Selberg)
↓ Ramanujan's formulas for $1/\pi$ fall out

Every step in this chain is a theorem. The deepest ingredient is the Jugendtraum result — that CM torsion generates abelian extensions — which requires the full machinery of class field theory (Artin reciprocity, idèles) to prove properly. But the statement is beautifully concrete: a single transcendental number, $j(\tau)$, knows the arithmetic of the class group of an imaginary quadratic field.

Deep Dive: The Artin Map and Frobenius

The Artin map makes class field theory explicit. For an ideal $\mathfrak{a}$ of $\mathcal{O}_K$ coprime to the conductor, the Artin symbol $\left(\frac{K^{ab}/K}{\mathfrak{a}}\right)$ is the automorphism of $K^{ab}/K$ corresponding to $\mathfrak{a}$ under the reciprocity isomorphism.

For the CM curve, the Artin symbol acting on a torsion point $P \in E[n]$ via the $n$-torsion Galois action equals the action of the endomorphism $[\alpha]$ for $\alpha$ a generator of $\mathfrak{a}$ — after reducing mod $n$. This is the precise statement that "Galois acts like endomorphisms" for CM curves.

At a prime $\mathfrak{p}$ of $K$ above a rational prime $p$: the Frobenius $\text{Frob}_{\mathfrak{p}}$ acts on $E[\ell]$ as the CM endomorphism $[\pi_\mathfrak{p}]$ where $\pi_\mathfrak{p}$ is a uniformiser (a "Hecke Grössencharacter"). This is why the number of points $|E(\mathbb{F}_p)|$ can be computed as $p + 1 - (\pi_\mathfrak{p} + \bar{\pi}_\mathfrak{p})$ — it is determined by the CM structure alone.

Deep Dive: The Chudnovsky Algorithm

The Chudnovsky brothers (1988) turned Ramanujan's ideas into a practical algorithm. Their formula (using $d = 163$):

$$\frac{1}{\pi} = \frac{12}{(640320)^{3/2}} \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (6n)!}{(3n)!(n!)^3} \cdot \frac{13591409 + 545140134\,n}{640320^{3n}}$$

Here $640320^3 = 262537412640768000 = |j(\tau)|$ for $\tau = \frac{1+\sqrt{-163}}{2}$. Each term adds $\approx 14.18$ decimal digits. This is the basis for most world-record $\pi$ computations.

The constants $13591409$ and $545140134$ arise from the leading coefficients of the modular equation relating $j(\tau)$ and $j(163\tau)$ — pure CM arithmetic, nothing magical.

Why only 9 class-number-1 discriminants? Gauss conjectured this; it was proved by Stark and Baker (1966–1967) using Baker's theorem on linear forms in logarithms — itself a deep result in transcendence theory. The finiteness is ultimately because imaginary quadratic fields "run out" of unique factorisation as $d$ grows. The largest Heegner number $d = 163$ corresponds to Ramanujan's most famous formula because $e^{\pi\sqrt{163}} \approx 262537412640768743.999999999\ldots$ — within $7.5 \times 10^{-13}$ of an integer.
