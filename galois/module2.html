Permuting Roots: the Galois Group

2.1 — Symmetries of a Quadratic

The roots of $x^2 - 2$ are $\sqrt{2}$ and $-\sqrt{2}$. They are related by complex conjugation — or equivalently, by the map $\sigma: \sqrt{2} \mapsto -\sqrt{2}$. Let's think about what $\sigma$ does to the whole field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) = \{a + b\sqrt{2}: a,b\in\mathbb{Q}\}$:

$$\sigma(a + b\sqrt{2}) = a - b\sqrt{2} \qquad (a, b \in \mathbb{Q})$$

This map is a field automorphism: it preserves addition and multiplication (check: $\sigma((a+b\sqrt{2})(c+d\sqrt{2})) = \sigma(ac+2bd + (ad+bc)\sqrt{2}) = ac+2bd - (ad+bc)\sqrt{2}$, which equals $\sigma(a+b\sqrt{2})\cdot\sigma(c+d\sqrt{2})$ ✓). It also fixes $\mathbb{Q}$ pointwise ($\sigma(a) = a$ for all $a \in \mathbb{Q}$).

The group of all automorphisms of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ that fix $\mathbb{Q}$ is: $\{e, \sigma\}$ — the identity and the conjugation. This is the Galois group:

$$\text{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})/\mathbb{Q}) = \{e, \sigma\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$$

Order 2, equal to the degree $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}):\mathbb{Q}] = 2$. This is always true for Galois extensions.

2.2 — The Galois Group: Definition

Let $K/F$ be a field extension. The Galois group is:

$$\text{Gal}(K/F) = \{\sigma: K \xrightarrow{\sim} K : \sigma \text{ is a field automorphism and } \sigma|_F = \text{id}\}$$

The group operation is composition. Key theorem: if $K/F$ is a Galois extension (normal + separable), then $|\text{Gal}(K/F)| = [K:F]$.

Galois extension: $K/F$ is normal (every irreducible polynomial over $F$ with one root in $K$ splits completely in $K$) and separable (no repeated roots — automatic over $\mathbb{Q}$). Equivalently: $K$ is the splitting field of some polynomial over $F$.

2.3 — A Cubic: $x^3 - 2$ and $S_3$

The three roots of $x^3 - 2$ are:

$$r_1 = \sqrt[3]{2} \approx 1.260 \qquad r_2 = \omega\sqrt[3]{2} \approx -0.630 + 1.091i \qquad r_3 = \omega^2\sqrt[3]{2} \approx -0.630 - 1.091i$$

where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$. The splitting field $K = \mathbb{Q}(r_1, \omega)$ has degree 6 over $\mathbb{Q}$.

An automorphism $\sigma \in \text{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q})$ is determined by where it sends $r_1$ and $\omega$:

  • $\sigma(r_1)$ must be one of $r_1, r_2, r_3$ (another root of $x^3-2$)
  • $\sigma(\omega)$ must be a primitive cube root of unity — either $\omega$ or $\omega^2$

This gives at most $3 \times 2 = 6$ automorphisms. Since $[K:\mathbb{Q}] = 6$, all 6 are realised. They form the symmetric group $S_3$, acting by permuting the three roots:

Element of $S_3$ Action on roots $\sigma(\omega)$
$e$ (identity)$r_1 \to r_1,\; r_2 \to r_2,\; r_3 \to r_3$$\omega$
$(12)$$r_1 \leftrightarrow r_2,\; r_3 \to r_3$$\omega^2$
$(13)$$r_1 \leftrightarrow r_3,\; r_2 \to r_2$$\omega^2$
$(23)$$r_2 \leftrightarrow r_3,\; r_1 \to r_1$$\omega$
$(123)$$r_1 \to r_2 \to r_3 \to r_1$$\omega$
$(132)$$r_1 \to r_3 \to r_2 \to r_1$$\omega$
The Galois group is the symmetry group of the roots. $\text{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2},\omega)/\mathbb{Q}) \cong S_3$. The six elements are exactly the six ways of rearranging the three roots — the six symmetries a rational observer can't distinguish.
Deep Dive: Why Do Automorphisms Permute Roots?

If $\sigma$ is a field automorphism fixing $\mathbb{Q}$, and $r$ is a root of $f(x) \in \mathbb{Q}[x]$, then $\sigma(r)$ is also a root of $f$. Proof: $f(r) = 0 \Rightarrow \sigma(f(r)) = \sigma(0) = 0$. But $\sigma(f(r)) = f(\sigma(r))$ since $\sigma$ fixes the rational coefficients and preserves ring operations. So $f(\sigma(r)) = 0$.

Therefore every automorphism sends roots to roots — it permutes them. Different automorphisms give different permutations (otherwise they'd agree on a generating set). So $\text{Gal}(K/F)$ injects into the permutation group of the roots.
