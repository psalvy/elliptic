Elliptic Curves over ℂ

3.1 — The Big Reveal

Here's the deep connection: every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{C}$ is a torus — specifically, it's isomorphic to a quotient $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ for some lattice $\Lambda$.

The bridge between the flat torus and the algebraic curve is the Weierstrass ℘-function:

$$\wp(z) = \frac{1}{z^2} + \sum_{\omega \in \Lambda \setminus \{0\}} \left(\frac{1}{(z-\omega)^2} - \frac{1}{\omega^2}\right)$$

This doubly-periodic meromorphic function satisfies:

$$(\wp')^2 = 4\wp^3 - g_2\wp - g_3$$

The map $z \mapsto (\wp(z), \wp'(z))$ sends the flat torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ into the elliptic curve $Y^2 = 4X^3 - g_2 X - g_3$ in $\mathbb{CP}^2$. This is an isomorphism.

3.3 — Winding Numbers

A line with rational slope $p/q$ on the fundamental domain closes up: it winds $p$ times around one cycle and $q$ times around the other, forming a closed geodesic.

3.4 — The ℘-Function as a Map

The Weierstrass ℘-function is doubly periodic with periods $\omega_1, \omega_2$:

$$\wp(z + \omega_1) = \wp(z), \quad \wp(z + \omega_2) = \wp(z)$$

This means $\wp$ is well-defined on the quotient $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$. It has:

  • A double pole at the origin (the "point at infinity" on the torus maps to $\mathcal{O}$ on the curve)
  • Even symmetry: $\wp(-z) = \wp(z)$ — each value is taken twice (reflecting the $y \mapsto -y$ symmetry of the curve)
Key insight: The equivalence between tori and elliptic curves is not just topological — it preserves the group structure. Addition of points on the curve corresponds to addition of complex numbers modulo the lattice. Lines on the lattice map to the secant construction from Module 2.
