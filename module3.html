Elliptic Curves over ℂ

3.1 — The Big Reveal: The Translator Function

Here is the deep connection: every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{C}$ is a torus. Specifically, it is isomorphic to a quotient space $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ (the complex plane folded over a lattice $\Lambda$).

To get from a flat 2D coordinate $z$ on the lattice to an $(x, y)$ coordinate on the algebraic curve, we need a "translator." The Weierstrass $\wp$-function is exactly this translator:

$$\wp(z) = \frac{1}{z^2} + \sum_{\omega \in \Lambda \setminus \{0\}} \left(\frac{1}{(z-\omega)^2} - \frac{1}{\omega^2}\right)$$

Because of the warp-around boundaries of the lattice, any function mapping the torus to a curve must be doubly-periodic (giving the same output on opposite edges of the fundamental domain). The $\wp$-function is perfectly built for this.

By an elegant result in complex analysis, this specific function and its derivative inherently satisfy a specific differential equation:

$$(\wp'(z))^2 = 4\wp(z)^3 - g_2\wp(z) - g_3$$

Notice the shape of that equation! If we let our $x$-coordinate be $\wp(z)$ and our $y$-coordinate be $\wp'(z)$, we perfectly recreate the structure of our elliptic curve $y^2 = 4x^3 - ax - b$ (the $4$ is just a scaling convention).

The map $z \mapsto (\wp(z), \wp'(z))$ takes a coordinate from the flat torus and embeds it perfectly onto the algebraic curve in $\mathbb{CP}^2$.

3.3 — Winding Numbers

A line with rational slope $p/q$ on the fundamental domain closes up: it winds $p$ times around one cycle and $q$ times around the other, forming a closed geodesic.

3.4 — The Grand Finale: Why the Group Law Works

The Weierstrass $\wp$-function is doubly periodic with periods $\omega_1, \omega_2$:

$$\wp(z + \omega_1) = \wp(z), \quad \wp(z + \omega_2) = \wp(z)$$

This means $\wp$ has an even symmetry: $\wp(-z) = \wp(z)$ — each value is taken twice, which geometrically reflects the $y \mapsto -y$ symmetry of the curve from Module 2.

The Key Insight: The equivalence between tori and elliptic curves is not just topological—it preserves the group structure.

Adding points on the flat $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ lattice is just standard, simple complex vector addition. Because the $\wp$-function perfectly translates the Torus to the Curve, it also translates the math happening on them.

If you pick three points on the flat torus that add up to zero ($z_1 + z_2 + z_3 = 0 \pmod \Lambda$), and you feed those three coordinates through the $\wp$-function, they become three points $P, Q, R$ on the elliptic curve that lie on a perfectly straight line.

The bizarre secant-line addition you performed in Module 2 is literally just the curve's distorted algebraic representation of standard vector addition happening on the flat torus.
