The Explicit Formula: Primes from Zeros

5.1 — The Von Mangoldt Function

To connect $\zeta$ to the primes, we need a function that counts prime powers with a convenient weight. Define:

$$\Lambda(n) = \begin{cases} \log p & \text{if } n = p^k \text{ for some prime } p, k \geq 1 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

This is the von Mangoldt function. It weights each prime power by $\log p$ (its "size") and ignores composite numbers. The associated counting function:

$$\psi(x) = \sum_{n \leq x} \Lambda(n) = \sum_{p^k \leq x} \log p$$

is the Chebyshev function $\psi(x)$. It grows approximately like $x$ (this is equivalent to the Prime Number Theorem: $\psi(x) \sim x$). Recovering $\psi$ from $\zeta$ will be the goal.

The key relation: the logarithmic derivative of the Euler product is a Dirichlet series for $-\Lambda$:

$$-\frac{\zeta'(s)}{\zeta(s)} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\Lambda(n)}{n^s}$$

Proof: $\log \zeta(s) = -\sum_p \log(1-p^{-s}) = \sum_p \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{p^{-ks}}{k}$. Differentiating: $\frac{\zeta'(s)}{\zeta(s)} = -\sum_p \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{\log p}{p^{ks}} = -\sum_n \frac{\Lambda(n)}{n^s}$.

This is the magic: the Euler product has "filtered out" all composite integers. Only prime powers appear in $-\zeta'/\zeta$. The question is: how do we extract $\psi(x)$ from this Dirichlet series?

5.2 — Perron's Formula: Integration as a Selector

The key tool is Perron's formula. For $c > 1$ and $x > 0$, $x \neq 1$:

$$\frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_{c - i\infty}^{c + i\infty} \frac{x^s}{s}\,ds = \begin{cases} 1 & x > 1 \\ \frac{1}{2} & x = 1 \\ 0 & 0 < x < 1 \end{cases}$$

A vertical-line integral acts as a step-function selector: it picks out $1$ when $x > 1$ and $0$ when $x < 1$.

Using this: multiply the Dirichlet series $-\zeta'/\zeta = \sum_n \Lambda(n) n^{-s}$ by $x^s/s$ and integrate term-by-term:

$$\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty} \left(-\frac{\zeta'(s)}{\zeta(s)}\right)\frac{x^s}{s}\,ds = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \Lambda(n) \cdot \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\!\!\left(\frac{x}{n}\right)^{\!\!s}\frac{ds}{s}$$

By Perron's formula, the integral picks out $\Lambda(n)$ when $n < x$ and $0$ when $n > x$. The left side therefore equals $\psi(x) = \sum_{n \leq x} \Lambda(n)$.

5.3 — Moving the Contour: Poles Become Primes

We now evaluate the contour integral $\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty} (-\zeta'/\zeta)(s) \cdot x^s/s \,ds$ by moving the vertical line to the left. As we cross poles of $-\zeta'/\zeta$, the residue theorem contributes one term per pole.

The poles of $-\zeta'/\zeta(s) \cdot x^s/s$ are at:

  • $s = 1$: $\zeta$ has a simple pole with residue $1$, so $\zeta'/\zeta$ has a simple pole with residue $-1$. The integrand has residue $x^1/1 = x$. This is the main term.
  • Each non-trivial zero $\rho$: $\zeta(\rho) = 0$ means $\zeta'(\rho)/\zeta(\rho)$ has a simple pole. The residue of the integrand is $x^\rho/\rho$. These are the oscillatory correction terms.
  • $s = 0$: contributes $-\log(2\pi)$.
  • Trivial zeros $s = -2k$: contribute $\sum_{k=1}^\infty x^{-2k}/(-2k) = \frac{1}{2}\log(1 - x^{-2})$ (a small correction).

5.4 — Riemann's Explicit Formula

Adding all residues gives the exact identity:

$$\boxed{\psi(x) = x - \sum_\rho \frac{x^\rho}{\rho} - \log(2\pi) - \tfrac{1}{2}\log(1 - x^{-2})}$$

where the sum runs over all non-trivial zeros $\rho$ of $\zeta$, ordered by $|\text{Im}(\rho)|$.

This is Riemann's explicit formula. It is not an approximation — it is an exact identity. The prime-counting function $\psi(x)$ is expressed precisely in terms of the zeros $\rho$.

For the conjugate pair $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2} + i\gamma$ and $\bar\rho = \tfrac{1}{2} - i\gamma$:

$$\frac{x^\rho}{\rho} + \frac{x^{\bar\rho}}{\bar\rho} = 2\,\text{Re}\!\left(\frac{x^{1/2+i\gamma}}{\tfrac{1}{2}+i\gamma}\right) = \frac{2\sqrt{x}\,\bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}\cos(\gamma\log x) + \gamma\sin(\gamma\log x)\bigr)}{\tfrac{1}{4}+\gamma^2}$$

So each zero pair contributes an oscillation of amplitude $\sim \sqrt{x}/\gamma$ to $\psi(x)$.

Primes emerge, composites cancel. The von Mangoldt function filtered out composites (via the Euler product). Perron's formula extracted the partial sum from the Dirichlet series. The residues at $\zeta$-zeros provide the exact corrections that turn the smooth main term $x$ into the irregular step-function $\psi(x)$.
