Riemann's Extension to ℂ

2.1 — Nine Pages That Reshaped Mathematics

In 1859, Bernhard Riemann submitted a short paper to the Berlin Academy of Sciences. Nine pages. Titled "Über die Anzahl der Primzahlen unter einer gegebenen Grösse" (On the Number of Primes Less Than a Given Magnitude). It would become the most influential paper in the history of number theory.

Riemann's central move: take Euler's $\zeta(s) = \sum n^{-s}$, which only makes sense for real $s > 1$, and extend it to complex $s$. The zeros of this extended function, it turns out, encode the distribution of every prime.

2.2 — Analytic Continuation

The Dirichlet series $\sum n^{-s}$ converges for $\text{Re}(s) > 1$ but diverges for $\text{Re}(s) \leq 1$. Does $\zeta$ simply cease to exist to the left of this boundary? No — the formula breaks down, but the function can be extended.

The key principle of complex analysis: if a function is analytic (complex-differentiable) on some region, there is at most one way to extend it analytically to any larger connected domain. So if we find any formula that agrees with $\sum n^{-s}$ for $\text{Re}(s) > 1$ and is analytic on a wider domain, that formula is the unique extension of $\zeta$.

Extension to $\text{Re}(s) > 0$ via the alternating series. Define the Dirichlet eta function:

$$\eta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^s} = 1 - \frac{1}{2^s} + \frac{1}{3^s} - \frac{1}{4^s} + \cdots$$

This alternating series converges for all $\text{Re}(s) > 0$ (by a generalised alternating-series test). It relates to $\zeta$ by subtracting the even-indexed terms twice:

$$\eta(s) = \zeta(s) - \frac{2}{2^s}\,\zeta(s) = \bigl(1 - 2^{1-s}\bigr)\zeta(s)$$

Therefore, for $\text{Re}(s) > 0$, $s \neq 1$:

$$\zeta(s) = \frac{1}{1 - 2^{1-s}} \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^s}$$

This formula is analytic for all $\text{Re}(s) > 0$ except at $s = 1$, where the denominator $1 - 2^{1-s}$ vanishes but $\eta(1) = \log 2 \neq 0$, giving a simple pole with residue $1$.

Extension to all of $\mathbb{C}$ via the integral representation. For $\text{Re}(s) > 1$, Riemann showed:

$$\zeta(s) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \int_0^\infty \frac{t^{s-1}}{e^t - 1}\,dt$$

By deforming the integration contour to a Hankel contour around the negative real axis, this integral converges for all $s \in \mathbb{C} \setminus \{1\}$, giving the unique meromorphic continuation of $\zeta$ to the entire complex plane.

2.3 — The Pole at $s = 1$

Near $s = 1$:

$$\zeta(s) = \frac{1}{s-1} + \gamma + O(s-1)$$

where $\gamma \approx 0.5772$ is the Euler–Mascheroni constant. The singularity is a simple pole with residue $1$. This single pole is the only singularity of $\zeta$ in all of $\mathbb{C}$ — everywhere else $\zeta$ is analytic.

2.4 — The Functional Equation

Riemann's deepest result: $\zeta(s)$ satisfies a perfect symmetry about the line $\text{Re}(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$. Define the completed zeta function:

$$\xi(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}s(s-1)\,\pi^{-s/2}\,\Gamma\!\bigl(\tfrac{s}{2}\bigr)\,\zeta(s)$$

The prefactors remove the pole and the trivial zeros (see below), leaving $\xi$ entire. Then:

$$\boxed{\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)}$$

The substitution $s \mapsto 1-s$ reflects the complex plane across $\text{Re}(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$. The zeros of $\xi$ — and therefore the non-trivial zeros of $\zeta$ — are symmetric about this critical line.

Deep Dive: Proof Sketch of the Functional Equation

Starting from the integral representation and applying the Poisson summation formula to the theta function $\theta(\tau) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty e^{-\pi n^2 \tau}$, one proves that $\theta(1/\tau) = \tau^{1/2}\theta(\tau)$. Writing $\zeta(s)$ as a Mellin transform of $\theta$, this theta-inversion gives the functional equation after splitting the integral at $\tau = 1$.

Concretely, the completed form $\pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\zeta(s) = \int_1^\infty (\theta(\tau)-1)/2 \cdot (\tau^{s/2} + \tau^{(1-s)/2})\,d\tau/\tau$, which is manifestly symmetric under $s \mapsto 1-s$.

2.5 — Trivial and Non-Trivial Zeros

The functional equation $\zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin(\pi s/2)\,\Gamma(1-s)\,\zeta(1-s)$ forces zeros at $s = -2, -4, -6, \ldots$ (where $\sin(\pi s/2) = 0$). These are the trivial zeros — they encode no information about primes.

All remaining zeros of $\zeta(s)$ are called non-trivial. The functional equation + the Euler product (which shows $\zeta(s) \neq 0$ for $\text{Re}(s) > 1$) force all non-trivial zeros to lie in the critical strip $0 < \text{Re}(s) < 1$. The Riemann Hypothesis says they all lie on $\text{Re}(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
