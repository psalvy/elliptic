The Critical Strip

3.1 — Where the Non-Trivial Zeros Must Live

In Module 2 we established that $\zeta(s)$ extends to all of $\mathbb{C}$ except $s=1$, and that non-trivial zeros must lie in the critical strip $0 < \text{Re}(s) < 1$. Let's see why they can't escape.

They can't be at $\text{Re}(s) > 1$: The Euler product $\zeta(s) = \prod_p (1-p^{-s})^{-1}$ converges absolutely and each factor is non-zero for $\text{Re}(s) > 1$. So $\zeta(s) \neq 0$ in this half-plane.

They can't be at $\text{Re}(s) < 0$: The functional equation $\zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin(\pi s/2)\,\Gamma(1-s)\,\zeta(1-s)$ maps zeros in $\text{Re}(s) < 0$ to zeros in $\text{Re}(1-s) > 1$. But we just showed there are none there — except at the trivial zeros $s = -2,-4,-6,\ldots$ where $\sin(\pi s/2) = 0$.

The non-trivial zeros are therefore confined to $0 \leq \text{Re}(s) \leq 1$. The functional equation further forces $\text{Re}(s) = 0$ and $\text{Re}(s) = 1$ to be zero-free (shown by Hadamard and de la Vallée-Poussin in 1896 — their proof of the Prime Number Theorem). So we have:

$$\text{All non-trivial zeros lie in the open strip } 0 < \text{Re}(s) < 1.$$

3.2 — Symmetry: Zeros Come in Quadruples

Two symmetries govern the location of non-trivial zeros:

  • Complex conjugation: Since $\zeta(\bar s) = \overline{\zeta(s)}$ (the coefficients $n^{-s}$ are real), zeros come in conjugate pairs: $\rho$ and $\bar\rho$.
  • Functional equation: $\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$ means zeros come in pairs $\rho$ and $1-\rho$.

Together: the four points $\rho, \bar\rho, 1-\rho, 1-\bar\rho$ are all zeros whenever any one is. These form a rectangle symmetric about both the real axis and the critical line $\text{Re}(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

If $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2} + i\gamma$ (on the critical line), then $1 - \rho = \tfrac{1}{2} - i\gamma = \bar\rho$, and the rectangle collapses to just the pair $\tfrac{1}{2} \pm i\gamma$.

3.3 — The First Zeros: Riemann's Calculation

In his 1859 paper, Riemann computed the first few non-trivial zeros and found them — to his apparent lack of surprise — on the critical line. Using the Riemann-Siegel formula (which he derived but never published in full), he found:

$n$ $\rho_n$ (on critical line) $\gamma_n = \text{Im}(\rho_n)$
1$\frac{1}{2} + 14.1347\ldots i$14.1347…
2$\frac{1}{2} + 21.0220\ldots i$21.0220…
3$\frac{1}{2} + 25.0109\ldots i$25.0109…
4$\frac{1}{2} + 30.4249\ldots i$30.4249…
5$\frac{1}{2} + 32.9351\ldots i$32.9351…

All on the critical line $\text{Re}(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, exactly as the Riemann Hypothesis predicts.

3.4 — The Riemann Hypothesis

Riemann stated his conjecture briefly, almost as an aside: "…es ist sehr wahrscheinlich, daß alle Wurzeln reell sind" ("it is very probable that all roots are real"), where he meant that all non-trivial zeros of $\xi(s)$ are real, equivalently all zeros of $\zeta$ lie on $\text{Re}(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

The Riemann Hypothesis (1859): All non-trivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$ lie on the critical line $\text{Re}(s) = \frac{1}{2}$.

It is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems (Clay Mathematics Institute, \$1 million reward). It has been unproven for 167 years.

3.5 — Numerical Evidence

The first systematic numerical verification was done by Gram (1903), Backlund (1914), and Titchmarsh (1930s). Today:

  • The first $10^{13}$ non-trivial zeros have been verified to lie on the critical line (Platt, 2011)
  • Individual zeros have been found as high as $\text{Im}(\rho) \sim 10^{22}$ (Odlyzko)
  • No exception has ever been found

Yet the hypothesis remains unproven. The numerical evidence is overwhelming, but mathematics requires proof, not evidence.

Deep Dive: The Density of Zeros

How many zeros are there with $0 < \text{Im}(\rho) < T$? The answer is given by the Riemann-von Mangoldt formula:

$$N(T) = \frac{T}{2\pi}\log\frac{T}{2\pi} - \frac{T}{2\pi} + O(\log T)$$

The zeros become denser as $T$ grows, with average spacing $\sim 2\pi/\log T$ near height $T$. Near $T = 14$, the first zero, spacing is about $7$; near $T = 1000$, spacing is about $0.9$.

The density of zeros determines how accurate the explicit formula is — more zeros means finer control over the prime-counting function.
