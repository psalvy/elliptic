Zeros Determine Everything

4.1 — From Polynomials to Entire Functions

A degree-$n$ polynomial is completely determined by its $n$ roots (plus one scaling constant). That is, if I tell you that $p(x) = 0$ at $x = r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_n$ and $p(0) = c$, you can reconstruct $p$ exactly:

$$p(x) = c \cdot \frac{(x - r_1)(x - r_2) \cdots (x - r_n)}{(-r_1)(-r_2)\cdots(-r_n)}$$

Can the same be true for infinite-degree "polynomials" — i.e., entire functions? Hadamard's theorem says: yes, under a finite-order growth condition.

An entire function $f$ has finite order $\rho$ if $|f(z)| = O(e^{|z|^\rho})$ for large $|z|$. Polynomials have order $0$. The function $e^z$ has order $1$. $\sin z$ has order $1$.

4.2 — Hadamard's Product Theorem (1893)

Let $f$ be an entire function of finite order $\rho$, with zeros $z_1, z_2, z_3, \ldots$ (listed with multiplicity, $z_n \neq 0$). Then:

$$f(z) = z^m e^{g(z)} \prod_{n=1}^\infty E_p\!\left(\frac{z}{z_n}\right)$$

where $m$ is the order of zero at $z = 0$, $g(z)$ is a polynomial of degree $\leq \rho$, and $E_p$ is the Weierstrass elementary factor:

$$E_p(u) = (1-u)\exp\!\left(u + \frac{u^2}{2} + \cdots + \frac{u^p}{p}\right)$$

The key upshot: an entire function of finite order is completely determined by its zeros (plus the polynomial exponential factor $e^{g(z)}$, which encodes the growth rate, not more zeros).

Deep Dive: Why the Exponential Factors?

The infinite product $\prod (1 - z/z_n)$ might not converge if $\sum 1/|z_n|$ diverges. The exponential "compensating factors" $e^{z/z_n + z^2/(2z_n^2) + \cdots}$ are added to each term to force convergence without adding new zeros. For order-1 functions, we only need $E_1(u) = (1-u)e^u$, which is why the Hadamard product for $\xi$ takes the form it does.

For the sine function: $\sin \pi z = \pi z \prod_{n \neq 0} (1 - z/n)e^{z/n} = \pi z \prod_{n=1}^\infty (1 - z^2/n^2)$. The paired $\pm n$ zeros allow the simpler form — exactly Euler's product from Module 1.

4.3 — The Hadamard Product for $\xi(s)$

The completed zeta function $\xi(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}s(s-1)\pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\zeta(s)$ is an entire function of order $1$ (growth bounded by $e^{C|s|\log|s|}$, a bit faster than order $1$ but satisfying the product theorem). Its zeros are exactly the non-trivial zeros $\rho$ of $\zeta$.

The Hadamard product:

$$\xi(s) = e^{A + Bs} \prod_\rho \left(1 - \frac{s}{\rho}\right) e^{s/\rho}$$

where $A, B$ are computable constants ($B = -\sum_\rho \text{Re}(1/\rho) \approx -0.0231$) and the product runs over all non-trivial zeros $\rho$.

This is not an approximation. It is an exact identity — $\xi(s)$ is completely and uniquely determined by the zeros $\{\rho\}$. To understand $\zeta$, you must understand its zeros.

4.4 — The Logarithmic Derivative

Taking the logarithmic derivative of the Hadamard product gives an expansion for $\zeta'/\zeta$ in terms of the zeros:

$$\frac{\zeta'(s)}{\zeta(s)} = B - \frac{1}{s-1} + \frac{1}{2}\log\pi - \frac{1}{2}\frac{\Gamma'(s/2)}{\Gamma(s/2)} + \sum_\rho \left(\frac{1}{s-\rho} + \frac{1}{\rho}\right)$$

Each zero $\rho$ contributes a simple pole to $\zeta'/\zeta$. This sum will be the key object in Module 5, where Perron's formula extracts the prime-counting function by integrating $-\zeta'/\zeta$ along a vertical line.

4.5 — Parallel with the Elliptic Course

If you followed the elliptic curve course, this should feel familiar. There, the $j$-invariant was the "fingerprint" of a torus — knowing $j(\tau)$ determined the torus up to isomorphism. Here, the zeros $\{\rho\}$ are the fingerprint of $\zeta$ — they determine $\zeta$ up to the exponential factor.

In both cases, a discrete set of "special values" (CM points giving integer $j$-values; non-trivial zeros) encodes global arithmetic information (class numbers; prime distribution). The Hadamard product is the analytic mechanism by which the local data (zero locations) reconstructs the global function.
