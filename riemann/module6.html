The Prime Number Theorem and the Riemann Hypothesis

6.1 — From the Explicit Formula to the PNT

The explicit formula from Module 5:

$$\psi(x) = x - \sum_\rho \frac{x^\rho}{\rho} - \log(2\pi) - \tfrac{1}{2}\log(1-x^{-2})$$

is exact. But for the Prime Number Theorem — $\pi(x) \sim x/\log x$, or equivalently $\psi(x) \sim x$ — we only need the leading behaviour. The question becomes: how large are the zero terms $\sum x^\rho/\rho$?

Each term $x^\rho/\rho$ has magnitude $|x^\rho/\rho| = x^{\text{Re}(\rho)} / |\rho|$. If all zeros had $\text{Re}(\rho) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, every term would have magnitude $\sqrt{x}/|\rho|$, giving a total oscillation of size $O(\sqrt{x}\log^2 x)$ — negligible compared to $x$.

But to prove even $\psi(x) \sim x$, we only need the weaker statement: there are no zeros with $\text{Re}(\rho) = 1$. This is what Hadamard and de la Vallée-Poussin proved in 1896.

6.2 — The Zero-Free Region and the PNT

Theorem (Hadamard, de la Vallée-Poussin, 1896): $\zeta(1 + it) \neq 0$ for all real $t \neq 0$.

The proof uses a beautiful trigonometric inequality: $3 + 4\cos\theta + \cos 2\theta \geq 0$ for all $\theta$. If $\zeta$ had a zero at $1 + it_0$, the Euler product would force a logarithmic singularity that contradicts this inequality via the identity $\text{Re}(-\zeta'/\zeta(\sigma)) \geq 0$ and the behaviour of $\zeta$ near $s = 1$.

Once we know zeros are bounded away from $\text{Re}(s) = 1$, we can estimate the sum in the explicit formula and show $\psi(x)/x \to 1$. Equivalently:

$$\boxed{\pi(x) \sim \frac{x}{\log x}} \qquad \text{(Prime Number Theorem)}$$

6.3 — A Better Approximation: $\text{Li}(x)$

The logarithmic integral $\text{Li}(x) = \int_2^x dt/\log t$ is a better approximation to $\pi(x)$ than $x/\log x$. In the explicit formula language, the main term at $s = 1$ contributes $\text{Li}(x)$ to $\pi(x)$ (after accounting for the change of variables from $\psi$ to $\pi$):

$$\pi(x) = \text{Li}(x) - \sum_\rho \text{Li}(x^\rho) + O\bigl(\sqrt{x}\,e^{-c\sqrt{\log x}}\bigr)$$

where the sum is over non-trivial zeros. Each zero contributes a term $\text{Li}(x^\rho) \approx x^{\text{Re}(\rho)} / (|\rho| \log x)$.

6.4 — What the Riemann Hypothesis Implies

If all non-trivial zeros have $\text{Re}(\rho) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, each zero contribution to $\pi(x)$ has magnitude $\sim x^{1/2}/(|\rho| \log x)$. The error term becomes:

$$\pi(x) = \text{Li}(x) + O\bigl(\sqrt{x}\,\log x\bigr)$$

This is the best possible error bound consistent with the prime distribution (no square-root cancellation can do better given that zeros have real part $\tfrac{1}{2}$). The Riemann Hypothesis says: the primes are as uniformly distributed as they could possibly be — no unexpected clumping or gaps beyond what $\sqrt{x}\log x$ allows.

Conversely, if any zero had $\text{Re}(\rho) = \sigma_0 > \tfrac{1}{2}$, that zero would contribute an error term of size $x^{\sigma_0}/(\text{something})$, noticeably larger than $\sqrt{x}$, revealing a subtle bias in the primes.

The Riemann Hypothesis in one sentence: The primes are as regular as possible — their fluctuations around $\text{Li}(x)$ are bounded by $O(\sqrt{x}\log x)$, no larger.

6.5 — Why It Matters

Beyond prime distribution, RH has hundreds of consequences across mathematics:

  • Arithmetic: RH implies sharp error bounds in the prime number theorem, Goldbach-type estimates, gaps between consecutive primes
  • Cryptography: Algorithms whose complexity analyses assume RH (e.g., primality testing, lattice problems)
  • L-functions: The Generalised Riemann Hypothesis (GRH) extends the conjecture to L-functions of all Dirichlet characters — consequences include bounds on least non-residues, zero-free regions for automorphic forms
  • Random matrix theory: Montgomery's conjecture (1973) says the pair correlation of zeros matches the eigenvalue statistics of random Hermitian matrices (GUE ensemble) — a mysterious link to quantum chaos

6.6 — The Road Forward

This course traced one arc: from Euler's Basel sum through Riemann's extension to the explicit formula and PNT. But the story continues in several directions:

  • L-functions of elliptic curves (see the elliptic curve course): the Birch–Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture predicts the rank of $E(\mathbb{Q})$ from the order of vanishing of $L(E,s)$ at $s=1$ — the exact same zero-counting philosophy applied to a richer object
  • Langlands program: a vast generalisation connecting Galois representations to automorphic forms and their L-functions — the frontier of 21st-century number theory
  • Modularity (Wiles 1995): all elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$ are modular, meaning their L-functions are L-functions of modular forms — which have their own Euler products, functional equations, and zeros

Every thread leads back to the same structure: an L-function, a functional equation, non-trivial zeros, and the question of where those zeros live.

Li(x) is remarkably accurate. At $x = 1000$, $\pi(1000) = 168$, $\text{Li}(1000) \approx 177.6$, $1000/\log 1000 \approx 144.8$.
