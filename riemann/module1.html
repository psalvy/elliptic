The Sum That Started Everything

1.1 — Euler's Solution to the Basel Problem

In 1644, Pietro Mengoli asked a deceptively simple question: what is the exact value of the sum of the reciprocals of all perfect squares?

$$\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2} = 1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} + \frac{1}{16} + \frac{1}{25} + \cdots = \;?$$

The series clearly converges (partial sums approach ≈ 1.6449) but no one could find what it converged to. The problem sat open for 90 years. In 1734, Leonhard Euler, aged 26, solved it — and opened a door that would lead directly to the deepest questions in mathematics.

Euler's key insight: the sine function has zeros at every integer $x = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots$, so, just as a polynomial is determined by its roots, the sine function has an infinite product representation:

$$\sin(\pi x) = \pi x \prod_{n=1}^\infty \!\left(1 - \frac{x^2}{n^2}\right)$$

The $\pi x$ factor accounts for the zero at $x = 0$ with the correct slope; the $(1 - x^2/n^2)$ factors pair up the symmetric zeros at $\pm n$. Dividing by $\pi x$:

$$\frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x} = \prod_{n=1}^\infty \!\left(1 - \frac{x^2}{n^2}\right) = 1 - \left(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2}\right)x^2 + \cdots$$

The left side has a perfectly ordinary Taylor expansion:

$$\frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x} = 1 - \frac{\pi^2 x^2}{6} + \frac{\pi^4 x^4}{120} - \cdots$$

Matching the coefficient of $x^2$ on both sides:

$$\boxed{\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6}}$$
The Basel problem is solved. Euler also found $\sum 1/n^4 = \pi^4/90$, $\sum 1/n^6 = \pi^6/945$, and all even powers — each a rational multiple of the corresponding power of $\pi$. The integers are woven into $\pi$ in a completely unexpected way.
Deep Dive: Is Euler's Product Rigorous?

Euler's argument was criticised at the time as non-rigorous: he assumed the sine function has a product expansion in the same way a polynomial does, without proof. This gap was later closed by Weierstrass (1876) and more generally by Hadamard (1893), whose product theorem we will meet in Module 4.

The key condition is that $\sin(\pi x) / (\pi x)$ is an entire function of finite order, which ensures the product converges and the coefficient comparison is valid. Euler's intuition was correct — he just lacked the 19th-century machinery to make it precise.

A rigorous modern proof of $\zeta(2) = \pi^2/6$ uses Parseval's identity: for $f(x) = x$ on $[-\pi, \pi]$, the Fourier series gives $\sum_{n=1}^\infty 1/n^2 = \pi^2/6$ directly.

1.2 — The Riemann Zeta Function

Euler's trick works for any exponent. Replace $2$ with any real number $s > 1$:

$$\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s} = 1 + \frac{1}{2^s} + \frac{1}{3^s} + \frac{1}{4^s} + \cdots$$

This is the Riemann zeta function. The condition $s > 1$ ensures convergence. At $s = 1$, the sum is the harmonic series $1 + \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{3} + \cdots$, which diverges. Special values:

1.3 — The Euler Product: A Bridge to the Primes

$\zeta(s)$ looks like a sum over all integers. But Euler discovered it can be written as a product over primes alone — the key bridge from analysis to number theory.

For each prime $p$, factor out its contribution by observing:

$$\frac{1}{1-p^{-s}} = 1 + p^{-s} + p^{-2s} + p^{-3s} + \cdots = \sum_{k=0}^\infty p^{-ks}$$

Multiply over all primes. By the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic (unique factorisation), every positive integer $n = 2^{a_2} 3^{a_3} 5^{a_5} \cdots$ appears in this product exactly once, as the term $2^{-a_2 s} \cdot 3^{-a_3 s} \cdots = n^{-s}$. Therefore:

$$\zeta(s) = \prod_{p\,\text{prime}} \frac{1}{1 - p^{-s}}$$

This is the Euler product formula. It encodes the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic in a single equation — and it means that to understand $\zeta(s)$, you need to understand how the primes distribute.

Deep Dive: The Sieve Derivation

Here is the cleanest proof of the Euler product. Start by multiplying $\zeta(s)$ by $2^{-s}$:

$$2^{-s}\zeta(s) = \frac{1}{2^s} + \frac{1}{4^s} + \frac{1}{6^s} + \frac{1}{8^s} + \cdots$$

Subtracting kills all even-$n$ terms:

$$(1-2^{-s})\zeta(s) = 1 + \frac{1}{3^s} + \frac{1}{5^s} + \frac{1}{7^s} + \cdots$$

Multiply by $3^{-s}$ and subtract again — kills all remaining multiples of 3. Continue for every prime $p$. By the Fundamental Theorem, after sieving out all primes, only the $n=1$ term survives:

$$\prod_p (1 - p^{-s}) \cdot \zeta(s) = 1 \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad \zeta(s) = \prod_p \frac{1}{1-p^{-s}}$$

1.4 — Infinitely Many Primes (Euler's Proof)

Euclid proved infinitely many primes by contradiction (assume finitely many, derive absurdity). The Euler product gives a more quantitative version:

At $s = 1$: $\zeta(1) = \sum 1/n = \infty$ (harmonic series). But the Euler product at $s=1$ would be $\prod_p (1-1/p)^{-1}$. If there were only finitely many primes, this finite product would be finite — contradiction. So there are infinitely many primes.

More precisely, taking logarithms: $\log \zeta(s) = -\sum_p \log(1 - p^{-s}) \approx \sum_p p^{-s}$ as $s \to 1^+$. Since the left side diverges, the sum $\sum_p p^{-s}$ diverges — which means there are not only infinitely many primes, but they are "thick enough" in the integers for this sum to blow up. This observation seeds everything that follows.
