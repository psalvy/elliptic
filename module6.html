Into the Weeds: Deriving the Formula

In Module 5, we saw Ramanujan's terrifyingly precise formula. But where does $\pi$ actually come from? And why is there a sum of factorials? To derive this, we need to look at the calculus of elliptic curves. The derivation relies on four "gears" turning together.

Gear 1: The Origin of π (Legendre's Relation)

If we want to calculate $\pi$, we need an equation that inherently contains $\pi$. In elliptic curve theory, $\pi$ is hiding in the curve's periods.

Recall that an elliptic curve is made by folding a lattice with periods $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$. In calculus, there are also "quasi-periods" $\eta_1$ and $\eta_2$ associated with the integrals of the second kind (measuring the surface area/flux). In 1811, Legendre proved a fundamental topological law relating these four numbers:

$$\omega_1 \eta_2 - \omega_2 \eta_1 = 2\pi i$$

This is the anchor. If we can calculate $\omega$ and $\eta$, we can isolate $\pi$.

Gear 2: Calculating Periods (Hypergeometric Series)

How do we actually compute the period $\omega$? By integrating the differential $dx/y$ over the elliptic curve $y^2 = 4x^3 - g_2x - g_3$.

Through a Taylor expansion of the integral, the period $\omega$ can be expressed as a Hypergeometric Series — an infinite sum built out of ascending factorials. For a specific curve parameterization (using a variable $x$ related to the $j$-invariant), the period looks like:

$$\omega \propto \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)_k \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)_k \left(\frac{3}{4}\right)_k}{(k!)^3} x^k$$

(Note: $(a)_k$ is the Pochhammer symbol, meaning $a(a+1)(a+2)\dots(a+k-1)$. This evaluates to heavily nested factorials like $(4k)! / (k!)^4$.)

Because $\eta$ is related to the derivative of $\omega$, the quasi-period $\eta$ is just a variation of this same sum, but with an extra "$k$" multiplied into the terms.

Gear 3: Complex Multiplication (The Algebraic Lock)

Normally, $\omega_1$, $\omega_2$, $\eta_1$, and $\eta_2$ are four completely independent, messy transcendental numbers. Legendre's relation ($\omega_1 \eta_2 - \omega_2 \eta_1 = 2\pi i$) is useless if you have four unknowns.

This is where Complex Multiplication (CM) saves us. Remember from Module 5 that for special Heegner numbers (like $d=58$ or $163$), the lattice has rotational symmetry. Mathematically, this forces $\omega_2$ to be an exact algebraic multiple of $\omega_1$. It also locks $\eta$ and $\omega$ together via algebraic constants!

The CM Miracle: At a CM point, the left side of Legendre's relation simplifies from a mess of four variables into exactly: $\omega^2 \times \text{(An Algebraic Number)} = \pi$

Gear 4: Clausen's Formula (Squaring the Sum)

Legendre + CM tells us that $\omega^2 \propto \pi$. But our series from Gear 2 only computes $\omega$. How do we square an infinite hypergeometric sum?

In 1828, Thomas Clausen proved a bizarre identity: the square of a hypergeometric series is just another, generalized hypergeometric series! When you square the series for $\omega$ and combine it with the derivative series for $\eta$, the result collapses into a single, unified infinite sum of the form:

$$\frac{1}{\pi} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(4k)!}{(k!)^4} (A + B \cdot k) X^k$$

This is the skeleton of Ramanujan's formula! The $(4k)! / (k!)^4$ comes from Clausen's hypergeometric squaring. The $(A + B \cdot k)$ comes from combining the $\omega$ (constant) and $\eta$ (derivative, $k$) series. The $X$ is related to $1/j(\tau)$.
