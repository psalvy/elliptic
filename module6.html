Into the Weeds: The Rigorous Derivation

In Module 5, we saw the formula. Now, we derive it. To get from a torus to Ramanujan's $\pi$ formula, we must execute four distinct mathematical steps: evaluating the elliptic period via Taylor series, squaring it, introducing $\pi$ via topology, and locking it all down with Complex Multiplication.

Step 1: The Period as a Taylor Series

To see the exact calculus of how an elliptic period becomes an infinite sum of factorials, let's map our elliptic curve into its Legendre Normal Form: $y^2 = x(1-x)(1-zx)$.

The fundamental real period $\omega$ is the integral between the roots (from $x=0$ to $x=1$):

$$\omega = \int_0^1 \frac{dx}{\sqrt{x(1-x)(1-zx)}}$$

We can rewrite the denominator by splitting the square roots:

$$\omega = \int_0^1 x^{-1/2} (1-x)^{-1/2} (1-zx)^{-1/2} \, dx$$

Now, we use Isaac Newton's generalized binomial theorem to expand the rightmost term into an infinite Taylor series:

$$(1 - zx)^{-1/2} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k}{k!} (zx)^k$$

(Note: $(a)_k$ is the Pochhammer symbol, representing a rising factorial: $a(a+1)(a+2)\dots(a+k-1)$.)

We substitute this infinite sum back into our integral. Because the series converges uniformly, we are allowed to swap the integral and the infinite sum, pulling everything that doesn't depend on $x$ completely outside the integral:

$$\omega = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k}{k!} z^k \left( \int_0^1 x^{k-1/2} (1-x)^{-1/2} \, dx \right)$$

We have successfully isolated $x$! The integral inside the parentheses is famous: it is exactly the definition of the Euler Beta Function, $B(k+1/2, 1/2)$.

The Beta function evaluates perfectly into Euler Gamma functions ($\Gamma$), which in turn map directly back to Pochhammer factorials ($\Gamma(k+1/2) = (\frac{1}{2})_k \sqrt{\pi}$):

$$B\left(k+\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{\Gamma(k+\frac{1}{2})\Gamma(\frac{1}{2})}{\Gamma(k+1)} = \frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k \sqrt{\pi} \cdot \sqrt{\pi}}{k!} = \pi \frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k}{k!}$$

We plug this beautifully simplified result back into our infinite sum, and the two $(\frac{1}{2})_k$ terms multiply together:

$$\omega = \pi \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k (\frac{1}{2})_k}{(k!)^2} z^k$$

Because infinite sums built out of rising factorials appear everywhere in differential equations, mathematicians gave them a template called a Hypergeometric Series ($_2F_1$). Our result perfectly matches that template!

$$\omega = \pi \cdot {}_2F_1\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}; 1; z\right)$$

By applying a slightly different algebraic transformation to the roots of the curve, this exact same calculus process generates the $_2F_1(\frac{1}{4}, \frac{3}{4}; 1; z)$ variant that Ramanujan used.

Step 2: The Hypergeometric Function & Clausen's Identity

Because infinite sums built out of rising factorials appear everywhere in differential equations, mathematicians gave them a name: Hypergeometric Series. The standard form is $_2F_1$, which takes two top parameters ($a, b$) and one bottom parameter ($c$):

$$ _2F_1(a, b; c; z) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(a)_k (b)_k}{(c)_k k!} z^k $$

Looking at our integration result from Step 1, it matches this template perfectly! Therefore, we can write the period elegantly as $\omega = \pi \cdot {}_2F_1(\frac{1}{4}, \frac{3}{4}; 1; z)$.

Ramanujan's formulas don't use $\omega$; they use $\omega^2$. In 1828, Thomas Clausen discovered a miraculous identity: squaring a $_2F_1$ series produces a generalized $_3F_2$ series.

$$\left[ {}_2F_1\left(\frac{1}{4}, \frac{3}{4}; 1; z\right) \right]^2 = {}_3F_2\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{4}, \frac{3}{4}; 1, 1; z\right)$$

If we unpack that $_3F_2$ series back into Pochhammer symbols, they expand into a massive ratio of standard factorials:

$$\frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k (\frac{1}{4})_k (\frac{3}{4})_k}{(1)_k (1)_k k!} = \frac{(4k)!}{(k!)^4 256^k}$$

Substituting this back into our squared period, we get the exact skeleton of Ramanujan's sum!

$$\omega^2 \propto \pi^2 \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(4k)!}{(k!)^4} \frac{1}{(j / 6.75)^k}$$

Step 3: Legendre's Relation (Injecting $\pi$)

We have a series for $\omega^2$, but we need an equation for $1/\pi$. This comes from the topology of the torus. Integrating around the fundamental parallelogram yields a residue, creating Legendre's Relation:

$$\omega_1 \eta_2 - \omega_2 \eta_1 = 2\pi i$$

To use this, we need an explicit formula for the quasi-period $\eta$. By the Picard-Fuchs differential equation, the quasi-period is fundamentally linked to the derivative of the real period. Specifically, $\eta$ can be written as a linear combination of $\omega$ and its derivative with respect to $z$:

$$\eta = C_1 \cdot \omega + C_2 \cdot \left( z \frac{d\omega}{dz} \right)$$

This explicit derivative is the secret engine of Ramanujan's formula! Look what happens when we apply the differential operator $z \frac{d}{dz}$ to our infinite series $z^k$:

$$z \frac{d}{dz} (z^k) = k \cdot z^k$$

Because $\eta$ contains the derivative of the series, a factor of $k$ drops down from the exponent. When we substitute $\omega$ (which has no $k$ multiplier) and $\eta$ (which has a $k$ multiplier) into Legendre's relation, the terms combine into a single infinite sum containing $(A + B \cdot k)$.

$$\frac{1}{\pi} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(4k)!}{(k!)^4} (A + B \cdot k) z^k$$

Step 4: Complex Multiplication (The Algebraic Lock)

We are almost there. We have the exact shape of the formula, but $A, B$, and $z$ are messy functions of $\tau$. We need them to be exact rational numbers.

We assume $\tau$ is in an imaginary quadratic field (e.g., $\tau = \frac{1 + \sqrt{-58}}{2}$). This gives the lattice Complex Multiplication (CM). Because the lattice maps onto itself, the second period $\omega_2$ is strictly proportional to $\omega_1$:

$$\omega_2 = \tau \omega_1$$

Substitute this into Legendre's Relation: $\omega_1 \eta_2 - \tau \omega_1 \eta_1 = 2\pi i$. Notice that $\omega_1$ factors out completely!

$$\omega_1 (\eta_2 - \tau \eta_1) = 2\pi i$$

Because of CM, the term $(\eta_2 - \tau \eta_1)$ evaluates to exactly $\omega_1$ multiplied by an algebraic constant $\alpha$. Therefore, the left side collapses entirely to $\alpha \cdot \omega_1^2$.

The Grand Conclusion: Complex Multiplication collapses Legendre's relation from four messy variables into exactly $\alpha \cdot \omega_1^2 = 2\pi i$. We substitute our infinite series for $\omega_1^2$ from Step 2, evaluate $j(\tau)$ for $d=58$, and the exact algebraic constants $A=1103$, $B=26390$, and $z = 1/396^4$ fall out flawlessly.

Part B: The Hypergeometric Skeleton.

This comes from Step 1 and Step 2. The binomial Taylor series of the elliptic integral yields a ${}_2F_1$ hypergeometric series. By Clausen's formula, squaring that series transforms it into a ${}_3F_2$ series, whose Pochhammer symbols expand exactly into $(4k)! / (k!)^4$.
Part C: The Linear Shift $(A + Bk)$.

This is the physical signature of the Picard-Fuchs differential equation from Step 3! Because Legendre requires $\eta$, and $\eta$ is explicitly defined using the derivative operator $z \frac{d}{dz}$, the exponent $k$ drops down into the coefficient, resulting in a constant term ($1103$) plus a derivative term scaled by the index $k$ ($26390k$).
Part D: The $j$-invariant Expansion $(z^k)$.

This comes from the substitution in Step 1. The parameter $z$ is proportional to $1/j(\tau)$. For the CM lattice $d=58$, the $j$-invariant is a massive exact integer, resulting in a denominator of $396^4$. Because this is in the denominator, each $k$ step shrinks the term exponentially, locking in ~8 decimal digits of $\pi$ per loop!
