5.2 — Complex Multiplication

Most lattices $\Lambda \subset \mathbb{C}$ only have trivial symmetries: $z \mapsto -z$ (and scaling). But for very specific choices — like $\tau = i$ (a perfect square) or $\tau = e^{2\pi i / 3}$ (a perfect hexagon) — the lattice can be mapped perfectly onto itself via rotation and complex scaling.

If $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\tau$ and there exists $\alpha \notin \mathbb{Z}$ with $\alpha\Lambda \subseteq \Lambda$, then $\tau$ satisfies a quadratic equation, placing it in an imaginary quadratic field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$. This "extra" symmetry is called Complex Multiplication (CM).

$$\text{Example: } \tau = i \implies \Lambda = \mathbb{Z}[i], \quad \tau = \frac{-1+\sqrt{-3}}{2} \implies \Lambda = \mathbb{Z}[\rho]$$

The profound consequence of CM is that the $j$-invariant is no longer a messy decimal — it is an exact algebraic integer:

$j(i) = 1728$ (exactly)
$j(\rho) = 0$ (exactly)
$j(\sqrt{-2}) = 8000$
$j\!\left(\frac{1+\sqrt{-163}}{2}\right) = -262537412640768000$ (exactly!)

5.3 — The CM Lattice & Heegner Numbers

The lattice $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\cdot\frac{1+\sqrt{-d}}{2}$ produces a torus whose shape is determined by $d$. For $d = 163$, $\operatorname{Im}(\tau) \approx 6.38$, giving an extremely "tall" fundamental domain and a very thin torus:

CM torus — drag to rotate

These values of $d$ are exactly the Heegner numbers — imaginary quadratic fields with class number 1. The near-integer $e^{\pi\sqrt{163}} \approx 262537412640768743.999\ldots$ is explained by the $j$-invariant's $q$-expansion: because $|q| = e^{-\pi\sqrt{163}}$ is so tiny, the higher-order terms nearly vanish.

5.4 — Ramanujan's Series for $1/\pi$

Ramanujan possessed an otherworldly intuition for CM lattices. He looked at $\tau = \frac{1 + \sqrt{-58}}{2}$, calculated its massive integer invariant, and plugged it into the $q$-expansion to isolate $\pi$. In 1914 he stated (without proof!):

$$\frac{1}{\pi} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{9801} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(4k)!\,(1103 + 26390k)}{(k!)^4\, 396^{4k}}$$

Each term adds roughly 8 decimal digits of precision. The Chudnovsky brothers later found a variant using $d = 163$:

$$\frac{1}{\pi} = \frac{1}{426880\sqrt{10005}} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(6k)!\,(13591409 + 545140134k)}{(3k)!\,(k!)^3\,(-262537412640768000)^k}$$

giving about 14 digits per term.

5.6 — Why This Works

The conceptual chain:

  1. CM lattice with extra symmetry ($d = 58$ or $163$)
  2. $j$-invariant is an algebraic integer → exact algebraic relations
  3. Modular equation connecting $j$ to Eisenstein series and $\pi$
  4. The large value of $e^{\pi\sqrt{d}}$ makes the $q$-expansion converge extremely fast

The bigger $d$ is, the smaller $|q| = e^{-\pi\sqrt{d}}$ is, and the faster the series converges. Ramanujan's genius was recognising which CM values produced series with rational coefficients.

The geometry of lattices, the algebra of elliptic curves, and the analysis of modular forms all converge on a single transcendental number.
