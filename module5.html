Complex Multiplication & Ramanujan's π

5.1 — Complex Multiplication

Most lattices $\Lambda \subset \mathbb{C}$ only have the obvious symmetries: $z \mapsto -z$ (and scaling). But some lattices have extra endomorphisms — multiplication by elements beyond $\mathbb{Z}$.

If $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\tau$ and there exists $\alpha \notin \mathbb{Z}$ with $\alpha\Lambda \subseteq \Lambda$, then $\tau$ satisfies a quadratic equation, meaning $\tau$ lives in an imaginary quadratic field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$.

$$\text{Example: } \tau = i \implies \Lambda = \mathbb{Z}[i], \quad \tau = \frac{-1+\sqrt{-3}}{2} \implies \Lambda = \mathbb{Z}[\rho]$$

For these "CM curves," the $j$-invariant is an algebraic integer. Here's the remarkable fact:

$j(i) = 1728$ (exactly)
$j(\rho) = 0$ (exactly)
$j(\sqrt{-2}) = 8000$
$j\!\left(\frac{1+\sqrt{-163}}{2}\right) = -262537412640768000$ (exactly!)

5.2 — The CM Lattice for $d = 163$

The lattice $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\cdot\frac{1+\sqrt{-163}}{2}$ has $\tau = \frac{1+\sqrt{-163}}{2}$, with $\operatorname{Im}(\tau) \approx 6.38$. This extremely "tall" fundamental domain produces a very thin torus:

These values of $d$ are exactly the Heegner numbers — the imaginary quadratic fields with class number 1. The near-integer $e^{\pi\sqrt{163}} \approx 262537412640768743.99999999999925\ldots$ is no coincidence: it's explained by the $j$-invariant's $q$-expansion.

5.3 — Ramanujan's Series for $1/\pi$

In 1914, Ramanujan stated (without proof!) the extraordinary formula:

$$\frac{1}{\pi} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{9801} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(4k)!\,(1103 + 26390k)}{(k!)^4\, 396^{4k}}$$

Each term adds roughly 8 decimal digits of precision. The number 1103 connects to $j(\sqrt{-58})$, and 396 relates to the singular modulus. The Chudnovsky brothers later found an even faster variant using $d = 163$:

$$\frac{1}{\pi} = \frac{1}{426880\sqrt{10005}} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(6k)!\,(13591409 + 545140134k)}{(3k)!\,(k!)^3\,(-262537412640768000)^k}$$

giving about 14 digits per term.

5.5 — Why This Works

The conceptual chain is:

  1. CM lattice with extra symmetry ($d = 58$ or $163$)
  2. $j$-invariant is an algebraic integer → produces exact algebraic relations
  3. Modular equation connecting $j$ to Eisenstein series and $\pi$
  4. The large value of $e^{\pi\sqrt{d}}$ makes the $q$-expansion converge extremely fast

The bigger $d$ is, the smaller $|q| = e^{-\pi\sqrt{d}}$ is, and the faster the series converges. Ramanujan's genius was recognizing which CM values produced series with rational coefficients.

The full circle: We started with quotient spaces (Module 1), learned the group law on elliptic curves (Module 2), connected tori to curves via $\wp$ (Module 3), classified curves by $j$ (Module 4), and now see how special CM values of $j$ unlock formulas that compute $\pi$ at breathtaking speed. The geometry of lattices, the algebra of elliptic curves, and the analysis of modular forms all converge on a single transcendental number.
