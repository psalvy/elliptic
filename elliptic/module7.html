Why 163? Class Numbers and CM Fields

Module 6 used the CM point $\tau = \frac{1+\sqrt{-58}}{2}$ to pin $\lambda$ to an algebraic number, and the Chudnovsky formula — used by every modern $\pi$-computing record — uses the discriminant $-163$. Neither number is arbitrary. Both sit at "class-number-1" points of a specific algebraic structure. This module explains what that means and why the list ends at 163.

The CM Theorem: $j$ is Always Algebraic at CM Points

The first thing to know: all CM points give algebraic $j$. If $\tau$ satisfies a quadratic equation over $\mathbb{Z}$ — that is, $a\tau^2 + b\tau + c = 0$ for some integers $a,b,c$ — then $j(\tau)$ is automatically an algebraic integer.

The geometric reason: a CM lattice $\Lambda$ carries extra endomorphisms. For an ordinary torus, the only maps sending $\Lambda$ to itself are multiplication by integers ($\mathrm{End}(E) = \mathbb{Z}$). For a CM torus, multiplication by certain algebraic numbers also preserves $\Lambda$, so $\mathrm{End}(E)$ is an order in an imaginary quadratic field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$. This extra symmetry rigidly pins the torus to a specific, algebraically constrained point in the moduli space.

This result — that $j$-values of CM points generate the maximal abelian extensions of imaginary quadratic fields — is Kronecker's Jugendtraum ("dream of youth"), formalised as Hilbert's 12th Problem and solved by the theory of complex multiplication developed by Kronecker, Weber, Hilbert, Takagi, and Shimura.

Class Number = Degree of $j$ over $\mathbb{Q}$

The interesting question is not whether $j(\tau)$ is algebraic, but how complicated an algebraic number it is. The answer is the class number $h(-D)$ of the imaginary quadratic field.

For $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$ with ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$ and discriminant $D$, the class number counts the number of inequivalent ideal classes — it measures how far $\mathcal{O}_K$ is from having unique factorization. $h = 1$ means every ideal is principal (unique factorization holds); $h > 1$ means some ideals need more than one generator.

For CM theory this has a direct consequence. The $h$ inequivalent ideal classes correspond to $h$ non-isomorphic CM elliptic curves with that discriminant. Galois conjugation permutes them. So:

$$\left[\mathbb{Q}\!\left(j(\tau)\right) : \mathbb{Q}\right] = h(-D)$$

When $h = 1$ there is only one CM curve in its isomorphism class. Galois has nothing to permute, so $j(\tau)$ has no conjugates — it lies in $\mathbb{Q}$. Since it is also an algebraic integer, it lies in $\mathbb{Z}$.

$h(-D)$ What $j(\tau)$ is Example
$1$ A rational integer $j(i) = 1728$
$2$ A quadratic irrational (root of a degree-2 polynomial over $\mathbb{Q}$) $j(i\sqrt{5})$
$h$ Root of a degree-$h$ polynomial with integer coefficients

The 9 Heegner Numbers

Gauss observed in the Disquisitiones Arithmeticae (1801) that $h(-D) = 1$ is extremely rare, and conjectured there are only finitely many such fields. He found 9 candidates. The complete list of positive integers $d$ for which $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$ has class number 1 is:

$$d = 1,\ 2,\ 3,\ 7,\ 11,\ 19,\ 43,\ 67,\ 163$$

These are the Heegner numbers. Proving the list is complete took 150 years. Kurt Heegner gave a proof in 1952 that was initially dismissed as flawed; Alan Baker and Harold Stark independently gave accepted proofs in 1966–67 using Baker's theorem on linear forms in logarithms.

For each Heegner number the relevant CM point is $\tau_d = \frac{-1 + i\sqrt{d}}{2}$, and $j(\tau_d) \in \mathbb{Z}$. The values are spectacularly simple — all perfect cubes:

Why the near-integers? — a quick derivation

The $j$ q-expansion is $j(\tau) = q^{-1} + 744 + 196884q + \cdots$ where $q = e^{2\pi i\tau}$. For $\tau_d = \frac{-1+i\sqrt{d}}{2}$:

$$q = e^{2\pi i \tau_d} = e^{\pi i(-1+i\sqrt{d})} = -e^{-\pi\sqrt{d}}$$

So $q^{-1} = -e^{\pi\sqrt{d}}$, and the expansion becomes:

$$j(\tau_d) = -e^{\pi\sqrt{d}} + 744 - 196884\,e^{-\pi\sqrt{d}} + \cdots$$

Since $j(\tau_d) \in \mathbb{Z}$ (call it $J$), rearranging gives:

$$e^{\pi\sqrt{d}} = 744 - J + 196884\,e^{-\pi\sqrt{d}} - \cdots \approx 744 + |J|$$

The correction term is $196884\,e^{-\pi\sqrt{d}}$. For $d = 163$: $e^{-\pi\sqrt{163}} \approx e^{-40.1} \approx 4.4 \times 10^{-18}$, so the correction is $\approx 8.7 \times 10^{-13}$ — giving 12 decimal nines. The larger $d$, the tinier the correction.

Why 163 Dominates

The Chudnovsky algorithm is:

$$\frac{1}{\pi} = \frac{12}{640320^{3/2}} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(6k)!}{(3k)!\,(k!)^3} \frac{13591409 + 545140134k}{(-640320^3)^k}$$

The ratio between successive terms is roughly $\frac{1728}{640320^3} \approx 6.6 \times 10^{-15}$, so each term adds approximately $\log_{10}(1.5 \times 10^{14}) \approx \mathbf{14.2}$ correct decimal digits of $\pi$. No other known series based on a Heegner number does better — because 163 is the last Heegner number, and a larger $d$ would give a tinier $|q|$ and more digits per term. The Heegner number list is provably finite and complete. 163 is its ceiling.

For comparison: the series based on $d = 67$ gives about 12 digits per term, and $d = 43$ gives about 9. The exponential growth of $|j|$ as a function of $d$ is why the progression 43 → 67 → 163 is so dramatic.

Why 58? — Level-4 Ring Class Numbers

For Ramanujan's $(4k)!/(k!)^4$ series (Module 6), the relevant modular function is not $j$ but the singular modulus $\alpha_r$, which lives on the modular curve $X_0(4)$. This is a level-4 modular function, symmetric under the congruence subgroup $\Gamma_0(4) \subset \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ — a strict subgroup of the full modular group.

For a level-4 function evaluated at a CM point $\tau = i\sqrt{r}$, the degree of the value over $\mathbb{Q}$ is the ring class number of a certain order inside $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-r})$ — specifically the order of conductor 2. This is generally a different (and larger) number than $h(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-r}))$.

For $r = 58$: the ordinary class number is $h(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-58})) = 2$, so $j(i\sqrt{58})$ is only a quadratic irrational. But the ring class number of the level-4 order at $r = 58$ equals 1, which forces $\alpha_{58} \in \mathbb{Q}$. The Borwein brothers proved this in Pi and the AGM (1987) and used it to verify all of Ramanujan's series.

The set of $r$ values for which Ramanujan's $(4k)!$ series has fully rational (integer) coefficients is a strictly larger collection than the 9 Heegner numbers, but still finite. Among these, $r = 58$ gives the fastest-converging series in that family.

The common structure: Both 163 and 58 are the largest values, at their respective levels of modular symmetry, for which a ring class number drops to 1. That single arithmetic condition — unique factorization in the relevant ideal class group — is what forces every algebraic irrational in the derivation to collapse into a clean rational integer.
