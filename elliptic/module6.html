Into the Weeds: The Rigorous Derivation

In Module 5, we saw the formula. Now, we derive it. To get from a torus to Ramanujan's $\pi$ formula, we must execute four distinct mathematical steps: evaluating the elliptic period via Taylor series, squaring it, introducing $\pi$ via topology, and locking it all down with Complex Multiplication.

Step 1: The Period as a Taylor Series

To see the exact calculus of how an elliptic period becomes an infinite sum of factorials, let's map our elliptic curve into its Legendre Normal Form: $y^2 = x(1-x)(1-\lambda x)$.

This form is obtained by applying a Möbius transformation that pins two of the four branch points (the values of $x$ where $y = 0$) at $x = 0$ and $x = 1$. The third finite root then lands at $x = 1/\lambda$. So $\lambda$ is not a root of the curve — it is a shape coordinate, a single number encoding where the third root sits relative to the first two. It carries exactly the same information as the $j$-invariant, just in different clothing:

$$j(\lambda) = 256\,\frac{(1 - \lambda + \lambda^2)^3}{\lambda^2(1-\lambda)^2}$$

As a function of the period ratio $\tau$, $\lambda = \lambda(\tau)$ is the modular lambda function. For a generic torus it is transcendental. But for a CM point like $\tau = \frac{1+\sqrt{-58}}{2}$, complex multiplication forces $\lambda(\tau)$ to be an algebraic number — a root of a polynomial with integer coefficients. That algebraic constraint is what ultimately allows everything to rationalize in Step 4.

Deep Dive: The Modular Lambda Function as a q-Series

If $\lambda(\tau)$ looks mysterious, a q-series expansion makes it concrete. Recall from Module 4 that the nome $q = e^{2\pi i\tau}$ converts the abstract period ratio $\tau$ into a single small complex number ($|q| \ll 1$ in the upper half-plane). The $j$-invariant has the famous expansion

$$j(\tau) = \frac{1}{q} + 744 + 196884\,q + 21493760\,q^2 + \cdots$$

$\lambda$ is a modular function of level 2 — it only has full symmetry under the index-6 congruence subgroup $\Gamma(2) \subset \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$, not the full modular group. Because of this restricted symmetry it is more sensitive to $\tau$, and it uses a half-nome $q_2 = e^{i\pi\tau}$ (so $q_2^2 = q$). Its expansion at the cusp $\tau \to i\infty$ is

$$\lambda(\tau) = 16\,q_2 - 128\,q_2^2 + 704\,q_2^3 - 3072\,q_2^4 + 11488\,q_2^5 - \cdots$$

Compare the two structures side by side:

$$j(\tau) = q^{-1} + 744 + 196884\,q + \cdots \qquad \lambda(\tau) = 16\,q_2 - 128\,q_2^2 + \cdots$$
  • $j$ has a pole at the cusp ($q \to 0$), reflecting the fact that every elliptic curve has a $j$-invariant but "most" of the moduli space lies near that pole.
  • $\lambda$ has a zero at the cusp ($q_2 \to 0$). This is because pinning two branch points at $0$ and $1$ leaves the third one ($1/\lambda$) flying off to infinity as the curve degenerates — so $\lambda \to 0$.

You can verify the $j$–$\lambda$ formula numerically: substitute the first few terms of $\lambda(\tau) = 16q_2 - 128q_2^2 + \cdots$ into

$$j = 256\,\frac{(1-\lambda+\lambda^2)^3}{\lambda^2(1-\lambda)^2}$$

and expand. The leading term $256/(16q_2)^2 = 256/(256\,q_2^2) = 1/q_2^2 = 1/q$ reproduces the pole of $j$ exactly.

For our CM point $\tau = \frac{1+\sqrt{-58}}{2}$, the nome $q_2 = e^{i\pi\tau}$ is exponentially tiny (since $\mathrm{Im}(\tau) = \frac{\sqrt{58}}{2} \approx 3.8$), so only a handful of terms are needed to compute $\lambda(\tau)$ to many decimal places — and CM guarantees the result is algebraic.

The fundamental real period $\omega$ is the integral between the roots $x = 0$ and $x = 1$:

$$\omega = \int_0^1 \frac{dx}{\sqrt{x(1-x)(1-\lambda x)}}$$

We can rewrite the denominator by splitting the square roots:

$$\omega = \int_0^1 x^{-1/2} (1-x)^{-1/2} (1-\lambda x)^{-1/2} \, dx$$

Now, we use Isaac Newton's generalized binomial theorem to expand the rightmost term into an infinite Taylor series:

$$(1 - \lambda x)^{-1/2} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k}{k!} (\lambda x)^k$$

(Note: $(a)_k$ is the Pochhammer symbol, representing a rising factorial: $a(a+1)(a+2)\dots(a+k-1)$.)

We substitute this infinite sum back into our integral. Because the series converges uniformly, we can swap the integral and the infinite sum, pulling everything that doesn't depend on $x$ completely outside the integral:

$$\omega = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k}{k!} \lambda^k \left( \int_0^1 x^{k-1/2} (1-x)^{-1/2} \, dx \right)$$

We have successfully isolated $x$! The integral inside the parentheses is exactly the definition of the Euler Beta Function, $B(k+1/2, 1/2)$.

The Beta function evaluates perfectly into Euler Gamma functions ($\Gamma$), which map directly back to Pochhammer factorials ($\Gamma(k+1/2) = (\frac{1}{2})_k \sqrt{\pi}$):

$$B\left(k+\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{\Gamma(k+\frac{1}{2})\Gamma(\frac{1}{2})}{\Gamma(k+1)} = \frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k \sqrt{\pi} \cdot \sqrt{\pi}}{k!} = \pi \frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k}{k!}$$

We plug this beautifully simplified result back into our infinite sum, and the two $(\frac{1}{2})_k$ terms multiply together:

$$\omega = \pi \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k (\frac{1}{2})_k}{(k!)^2} \lambda^k$$

Because infinite sums built out of rising factorials appear everywhere in differential equations, mathematicians gave them a template called a Hypergeometric Series (${}_2F_1$). Our result perfectly matches that template!

$$\omega = \pi \cdot {}_2F_1\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}; 1; \lambda\right)$$
Deep Dive: The U-Substitution & Defining $\alpha$

We just proved the period equals $\pi \cdot {}_2F_1(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}; 1; \lambda)$. But Ramanujan's formula uses a $(1/4, 3/4)$ series. Where did that come from?

Just like you can stretch the x-axis on a graph without changing the shape of a circle, you can apply a rational u-substitution to shift the roots of an elliptic curve without changing the physical 3D Torus. If we apply Gauss's Quadratic Transformation $x \mapsto \frac{4x}{(1+x)^2}$, the square roots in our integral get reshuffled.

This exact transformation yields a famous identity that perfectly shifts our series from halves into quarters:

$${}_2F_1\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}; 1; \lambda\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+\sqrt{\lambda}}} {}_2F_1\left(\frac{1}{4}, \frac{3}{4}; 1; \underbrace{\frac{4\sqrt{\lambda}}{(1+\sqrt{\lambda})^2}}_{\text{We define this as } \alpha}\right)$$

This identity explicitly defines our new parameter, $\alpha$.

Why do this? Because factorials of $(1/4)_k$ expand into $(4k)!$, whereas $(1/2)_k$ only expands into $(2k)!$. The $(4k)!$ variant grows astronomically faster, which is exactly what Ramanujan wanted to maximize the speed at which his formula calculates $\pi$!

Step 2: Clausen's Formula & The Singular Modulus

Ramanujan's formulas don't use $\omega$; they use $\omega^2$. We now work with the $(1/4, 3/4)$ variant of the period — recall from the Deep Dive above that Gauss's quadratic transformation shifts us from the $(1/2, 1/2)$ series to the faster-converging $(1/4, 3/4)$ one, at the cost of introducing the new parameter $\alpha$. In 1828, Thomas Clausen discovered a miraculous identity: squaring a ${}_2F_1$ series produces a generalized ${}_3F_2$ series.

$$\left[ {}_2F_1\left(\frac{1}{4}, \frac{3}{4}; 1; \alpha\right) \right]^2 = {}_3F_2\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{4}, \frac{3}{4}; 1, 1; \alpha\right)$$

Let's unpack that ${}_3F_2$ series back into standard factorials. The Pochhammer symbols expand perfectly, but they throw off an extra $256^k$ in the denominator:

$$\frac{(\frac{1}{2})_k (\frac{1}{4})_k (\frac{3}{4})_k}{(1)_k (1)_k k!} = \frac{(4k)!}{(k!)^4 256^k}$$

Now, we plug this back into our squared period. Notice that our new geometric parameter $\alpha^k$ combines with the factorial's $1/256^k$ to form $(\alpha / 256)^k$.

But what exactly is $\alpha$?

It is known as the Singular Modulus. Because our specific torus ($d=58$) has perfect Complex Multiplication, its extra geometric symmetries act as strict algebraic constraints. These constraints force the original curve parameter $\lambda$ to be the exact root of an integer polynomial. Because $\lambda$ is an exact algebraic root, our transformed parameter $\alpha = \frac{4\sqrt{\lambda}}{(1+\sqrt{\lambda})^2}$ is also an exact algebraic root.

By solving the modular polynomial equations for the $d=58$ symmetry, Ramanujan derived the exact algebraic root for $\alpha$. Miraculously, because of the specific structure of this imaginary quadratic field, when Ramanujan's root for $\alpha$ is divided by $256$, the nested square roots simplify entirely. It perfectly rationalizes into an astronomically tiny fraction:

$$ \frac{\alpha}{256} = \frac{1}{396^4} $$

This leaves us with the exact skeleton of Ramanujan's formula!

$$\omega^2 \propto \pi^2 \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(4k)!}{(k!)^4} \left(\frac{1}{396^4}\right)^k$$

Step 3: Legendre's Relation & The Missing Variables

We have an infinite series for $\omega_1^2$ (which we just called $\omega$ earlier). But how do we connect this to $\pi$? We must look at the topology of the torus.

Alongside the periods $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$, there are "quasi-periods" $\eta_1$ and $\eta_2$ created by integrating a different differential over the same loops. Integrating around the fundamental parallelogram of the torus yields a topological residue, creating Legendre's Relation:

$$\omega_1 \eta_2 - \omega_2 \eta_1 = 2\pi i$$

To use this, we need formulas for the quasi-periods. By the Picard-Fuchs differential equation, a quasi-period acts like a derivative of a period. Specifically, $\eta_1$ can be written as a combination of $\omega_1$ and its derivative with respect to our curve parameter:

$$\eta_1 = C_1 \cdot \omega_1 + C_2 \cdot \left( z \frac{d\omega_1}{dz} \right)$$

This explicit derivative is the secret engine of Ramanujan's formula! Look what happens when we apply the differential operator $z \frac{d}{dz}$ to an infinite series of $z^k$:

$$z \frac{d}{dz} (z^k) = k \cdot z^k$$

Because $\eta_1$ contains the derivative of the series, a factor of $k$ drops down from the exponent into the numerator.

The Roadblock: We now understand how to calculate $\omega_1$ and $\eta_1$. But Legendre's relation still contains $\omega_2$ and $\eta_2$! We have one equation and two unknown variables. We cannot isolate $\pi$ unless we find a way to eliminate the second loop of the torus.

Step 4: Complex Multiplication (The Algebraic Lock)

This is where Ramanujan's choice of $d=58$ saves the day. We assume $\tau$ is in an imaginary quadratic field (e.g., $\tau = \frac{1 + \sqrt{-58}}{2}$). This gives the lattice Complex Multiplication (CM).

Because the CM lattice has extra rotational symmetries, it maps perfectly onto itself. This geometric lock forces the second period $\omega_2$ to be a strict, exact multiple of the first period $\omega_1$:

$$\omega_2 = \tau \omega_1$$

Substitute this into Legendre's Relation: $\omega_1 \eta_2 - (\tau \omega_1) \eta_1 = 2\pi i$. Notice that $\omega_1$ factors out completely!

$$\omega_1 (\eta_2 - \tau \eta_1) = 2\pi i$$

The symmetry of Complex Multiplication doesn't just lock the periods; it also locks the quasi-periods. The term $(\eta_2 - \tau \eta_1)$ is mathematically guaranteed to evaluate to exactly $\omega_1$ multiplied by some exact algebraic constant, let's call it $C_{CM}$. Therefore, the unknown $\eta_2$ is absorbed, and the left side collapses entirely to $C_{CM} \cdot \omega_1^2$!

The Grand Conclusion: Complex Multiplication collapses Legendre's relation from four variables down to exactly $C_{CM} \cdot \omega_1^2 = 2\pi i$.

We substitute our infinite series for $\omega_1^2$ (from Step 2), and the derivative rules from the $\eta$ function (which injects the $+ Bk$ term). We evaluate the geometric parameters for $d=58$, and the exact algebraic constants $A=1103$, $B=26390$, and the singular modulus fraction $1/396^4$ fall out flawlessly.

This is the physical signature of the quasi-period from Step 3.
