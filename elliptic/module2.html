Elliptic Curves over ℝ

2.1 — The Weierstrass Form

An elliptic curve over the reals is defined by:

$$y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$$

The curve is non-singular (no cusps or self-intersections) exactly when the discriminant is nonzero:

$$\Delta = -16(4a^3 + 27b^2) \neq 0$$

2.2 — The Group Law: Point Addition

Elliptic curves have a remarkable group structure. Given two points $P$ and $Q$ on the curve:

  1. Draw the line through $P$ and $Q$. It intersects the curve at a third point $R$.
  2. Reflect $R$ over the $x$-axis to get $P + Q$.

When $P = Q$, use the tangent line instead (point doubling).

2.2b — The Algebra of Point Addition

To calculate $P + Q$ algebraically, we don't add the coordinates together. Instead, we translate the geometric steps (finding the secant line and its third intersection) into algebraic formulas. Let $P = (x_1, y_1)$ and $Q = (x_2, y_2)$.

Step 1: Find the slope ($m$) of the line.

  • If $P \neq Q$ (Point Addition): We just use the standard slope formula.
    $$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$
  • If $P = Q$ (Point Doubling): The points are the same, so we need the tangent line. We find this using implicit differentiation of $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$.
    $$m = \frac{3x_1^2 + a}{2y_1}$$

Step 2: Find the $x$-coordinate of the new point.

If we substitute our line equation $y = m(x - x_1) + y_1$ into our curve equation, we get a messy cubic polynomial. However, a brilliant algebra trick (Vieta's formulas) tells us that the sum of the three roots ($x_1, x_2, x_3$) must equal $m^2$. Therefore, finding the third $x$-coordinate is simply:

$$x_3 = m^2 - x_1 - x_2$$

Step 3: Find the $y$-coordinate and reflect.

We find the $y$-coordinate of the third intersection ($R$) by plugging $x_3$ back into the line equation. Finally, to get $P+Q$, we reflect it across the $x$-axis by flipping the sign:

$$y_3 = m(x_1 - x_3) - y_1$$
Deep Dive: The Unrolled Algebra of Point Addition

When we write the code for $P + Q$, we usually calculate the slope ($m$) as an intermediate variable. But what does the pure mathematical function look like if we unroll it completely in terms of our input coordinates $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$?

Assume $P \neq Q$ and the points do not form a vertical line.

1. The Explicit Slope:
The slope of the secant line passing through $P$ and $Q$ is:

$$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

2. The Explicit $x_3$:
Vieta's formulas tell us that the sum of the three roots $(x_1 + x_2 + x_3)$ equals $m^2$. Solving for $x_3$ and substituting our explicit slope gives:

$$x_3 = \left( \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \right)^2 - x_1 - x_2$$

3. The Intersection $y_R$:
The point intersects the curve on our secant line $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$. So, the $y$-coordinate of the intersection point ($R$) is:

$$y_R = \left( \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \right) (x_3 - x_1) + y_1$$

4. The Final Reflection $y_3$:
To get $P + Q$, we must reflect $R$ across the $x$-axis, meaning $y_3 = -y_R$. This gives us:

$$y_3 = -\left[ \left( \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \right) (x_3 - x_1) + y_1 \right]$$

Distributing the negative sign and flipping $(x_3 - x_1)$ to $(x_1 - x_3)$ to absorb it:

$$y_3 = \left( \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \right) (x_1 - x_3) - y_1$$

5. The "Monster" Equation:
Finally, let's plug our massive explicit $x_3$ definition into the $(x_1 - x_3)$ part of the $y_3$ equation:

$$y_3 = \left( \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \right) \left[ x_1 - \left( \left( \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \right)^2 - x_1 - x_2 \right) \right] - y_1$$

Simplify by combining the $x_1$ terms inside the bracket:

$$y_3 = \left( \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \right) \left[ 2x_1 + x_2 - \left( \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \right)^2 \right] - y_1$$

This rational function is exactly what the geometric operation of "drawing a line and flipping the point" is evaluating under the hood!

Check the math: Next time you drag $P$ and $Q$ on the graph, look at the coordinates in the green box. You'll see they exactly follow these polynomial rules, ensuring the resulting point is always perfectly locked onto the curve!

2.3 — Special Cases

Try these parameter values to see important curve shapes:

  • $a = -1, b = 0$ — curve passes through the origin, has two components
  • $a = -3, b = 2$ — singular! ($\Delta = 0$), the curve has a node
  • $a = 0, b = 0$ — singular cusp at the origin
  • $a = -1, b = 1$ — a nice smooth curve with one component
Key insight: The set of points on a non-singular elliptic curve, together with a "point at infinity" $\mathcal{O}$, forms an abelian group. This algebraic structure is what makes elliptic curves so powerful in number theory and cryptography.
